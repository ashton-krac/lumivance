Born into a light-skinned Creole family, Broyard moved with his family to Brooklyn during the Great Migration. While his family identified as Black, Broyard, upon returning from World War II, made the decision to pass as white. This choice allowed him to navigate the segregated literary world of New York City, where he became a renowned literary critic for *The New York Times*.

Broyard's career was marked by his wit, his sharp prose, and his ability to champion new voices in fiction. He was a fixture of the Greenwich Village intellectual scene, a charismatic figure who charmed the literary elite while guarding the secret of his heritage. It was only after his death that the full extent of his passing became public knowledge, revealing the immense psychological complexity of his life. Broyard had chosen to shed the "burden" of race to be judged solely as a writer, a decision that came at the cost of distancing himself from his family and his history.
