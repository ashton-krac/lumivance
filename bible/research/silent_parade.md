# The Silent Parade

The Silent Parade (or Silent Protest) was a march of between 8,000 and 10,000 African Americans in New York City on July 28, 1917. Organized by the NAACP, men, women, and children marched down Fifth Avenue to protest the East St. Louis riots and the rising tide of lynchings in the South. It was one of the first mass protests of its kind in American history.

The march was notable for its distinct lack of chanting or singing. The only sound was the muffled beat of drums. Men dressed in dark suits and women in white dresses, creating a striking visual contrast and an air of solemn dignity. They carried banners with slogans like "Your Hands Are Full of Blood" and "Make America Safe for Democracy." The silence was a deliberate tactic to showcase the discipline, humanity, and moral superiority of the protesters in the face of barbaric violence.
