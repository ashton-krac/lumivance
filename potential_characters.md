I checked the manuscript roster; these names do not appear in the chapters. I’d consider these 25 for post-2000 additions, especially if you want the Order’s modern wing to feel less like “celebrity cameo” and more like a full cultural operating system.

Zendaya — Youth glamour, surveillance-era innocence, Disney-to-Euphoria metamorphosis. She could expose how Lumivance grooms “acceptable ambiguity” for Gen Z screens.

Colin Kaepernick — Perfect for a flag/anthem counterpoint to Hendrix. The Order would want to control him; his refusal would make him dangerous.

Patrick Mahomes — A biracial quarterback as state ritual, military flyovers, betting markets, family branding, and American myth manufacture.

Stephen Curry — Use him for distance, probability, faith, and soft dominance. His three-point revolution is basically a mathematical coup disguised as joy.

Daveed Diggs — He belongs in any chapter about founding documents, Hamilton, Jefferson, performance, and rewriting the republic’s origin story in rhyme.

Keegan-Michael Key — Comedy as code-switching laboratory. He could be Jordan Peele’s lighter, more socially legible counterpart inside Lumivance narrative strategy.

Maya Rudolph — Impersonation, inheritance, SNL power, Kamala parody, and the Minnie Riperton lineage. She could weaponize mimicry.

Halsey — White-passing pop star as a modern Passing file. Useful for ambiguity, illness, fandom, sexuality, and the cost of being read incorrectly.

Amandla Stenberg — Youth activism, Black girl visibility, YA dystopia, and the politics of being made symbolic too young.

Yara Shahidi — Campus politics, youth voting, Harvard respectability, Iranian/Black identity, and polished civic optimism.

J. Cole — Anti-spectacle rap power. He would be useful as a quiet wealth/introspection node who distrusts Lumivance pageantry.

Logic — White-passing biracial identity made explicit through rap. Strong for a chapter about who gets believed when they claim Blackness.

H.E.R. — Hidden face, revealed voice. She fits protest music, anonymity, “I Can’t Breathe,” and the archive of masked witness.

Miguel — Desire, border identity, genre fusion, and Black/Latino creator economics. A sensual counter-frequency to Prince.

Anderson .Paak — Joy as survival tech. Drums, Korean/Black family history, Silk Sonic retro-performance, and working-class showmanship.

Tinashe — Independence from label systems, genre limbo, dance-body discipline, and the machine’s inability to market a mixed Black woman cleanly.

Zazie Beetz — Atlanta, Joker, Deadpool, German/Black identity. Good for uncanny modern media and transatlantic ambiguity.

Laura Harrier — Fashion, elegance, BlacKkKlansman, Spider-Man respectability optics. She could sit at the glamour/history intersection.

Elaine Welteroth — Teen Vogue, editorial power, biracial memoir, Black maternal health. Excellent for the media desk.

Pete Wentz — White-passing Black ancestry inside emo/pop-punk. He lets you show Lumivance entering white suburban adolescent melancholy.

Latto — Colorism, Southern rap, branding, and the burden of a former stage name tied directly to mixed identity.

Lauren London — Grief, Los Angeles, Nipsey Hussle, Jewish/Black identity, and community mourning after celebrity death.

Solange Knowles — She should probably be in the book. A Seat at the Table is practically a Lumivance counter-liturgical text.

Pharrell Williams — Producer as weather system: fashion, taste, Louis Vuitton, joy commerce, and the global supply chain of cool.

Janelle Monáe — Afrofuturism, android identity, queerness, surveillance, and liberation through constructed persona. If Lumivance has a future-facing myth lab, she belongs there.

Best immediate additions, if you only choose a few: Solange, Kaepernick, Zendaya, Daveed Diggs, Halsey, Elaine Welteroth, and Pharrell. They each pressure an existing chapter rather than merely adding another famous face.
