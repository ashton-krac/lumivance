# CHAPTER 18: THE REGENTS OF THE BLOOD

**DATE:** April 12, 1945  
**LOCATION:** Oval Office, White House, Washington D.C.  
**TIME:** 11:45 PM (Two hours after the death of Franklin D. Roosevelt)  
**PRESIDING:** Grand Architect Truman Gibson (The Landlord)  

**ATTENDEES (THE SHADOW CABINET):**  
1.  **Mary McLeod Bethune** - *The Matriarch*  
2.  **Robert Weaver** - *The Economist*  
3.  **Truman Gibson** - *The Architect*  
4.  **Harry S. Truman** - *The Tenant*  

---

**I. THE EMPTY ROOM**

"Jesus, Mary and Joseph," Truman muttered to himself, standing in the center of the Oval Office. 

"Pace? Yes, I should pace." 

He began to pace. 

Outside, the capital was dark, damp, weeping. The rain lashed against his window, a window he didn't feel was his. 

The news had broken two hours ago. Roosevelt was dead. The King was gone. And here he was, the Jester, wearing the crown that looked like it would slip over his ears and blind him.

Arriving at the window, he whispered to his reflection, "They're gonna eat you alive. Stalin. Churchill. They carve up maps with steak knives. I don't even know where the maps are kept. I don't know anything. He didn't tell me a damn thing. Not even about the... the thing in the desert. It burns the air? How do you burn air?"

The room felt immense, designed for a man in a wheelchair who filled space with his personality, his voice, his laughter. Without Roosevelt, the White House was just a drafty old house built on a swamp.

"I need... I need the Cabinet," rubbing his face with a clammy hand. "Edward. George. Why isn't anyone here? Where is the Service? Where are the guards?"

He walked to the double doors that led to the corridor. He reached for the brass handle, expecting the reassuring weight of an agent on the other side.

"Agent Starling?" he called out. "Agent... anyone?"

The handle turned easily. The door swung open.

The corridor was empty. No guards. No ushers. No military aides standing at attention. Just the long, carpeted hallway, stretching out into shadow, lined with the busts of dead guys who seemed to be watching him with marble contempt.

"Alright," and as he began to close the door, he heard it. Not footsteps. Not the martial click of boot heels. But instead, a slow, deliberate cadence.

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

Truman froze. The hair on his arms stood up. He knew that sound. Everyone in America knew that sound. It was the sound of the President moving. But the President was dead in Warm Springs, Georgia. His body was on a train.

"Mr. President?" Truman asked the ceiling.

The tapping grew louder. Closer. And then, emerging from the shadows of the East Wing, three figures appeared. They moved abreast, blocking the width of the hallway. They did not walk with the hurried deference of staffers. They walked with the slow, terrifying gravity of ownership.

In the center was a woman. She was short, heavy-set, her skin the color of deep mahogany. She wore a black velvet coat and a hat with a modest feather, clutching a heavy leather medical bag in one hand. In the other, she held the cane. It was FDR's cane. The blackthorn stick he had used for twenty years.

To her left was a man in a sharp, pinstriped suit, his face a mask of actuarial boredom. He carried a thick, leather-bound ledger.

To her right, a younger man, bespectacled, intellectual, holding a briefcase like a weapon.

Truman took a step back, retreating into the Oval Room. "Who are you?" he demanded, trying to find the voice of the Commander-in-Chief and finding only the squeak of the Vice President. "How did you get past my security detail? This is a restricted area. I am the President of the United States."

The three figures crossed the threshold. The man in the pinstriped suit reached out and closed the doors behind them. He locked it.

"Technically," the man said, his voice smooth and cold, like polished stone.

**II. THE INTRUSION**

Truman backed up until his legs hit the edge of the sofa. He sat down hard, the bourbon sloshing over his hand.

"I know you," Truman said, pointing a shaking finger at the woman. "You... you're the educator. Bethune. Mary McLeod Bethune. You run the... the Negro Affairs division at the NYA. My wife... Bess hates you."

Mary McLeod Bethune did not smile. She walked to the fireplace and placed the medical bag on the low table. She did it with a familiarity that shocked him—like she knew exactly where the table was, exactly how the light hit it.

"Bess is a woman of her time, Harry," Bethune said. Her voice was rich, warm, but underlaid with steel. "She fears what she does not understand. You, however, do not have the luxury of fear. Not anymore."

"And you," Truman shifted his gaze to the man with the ledger. "Truman Gibson. You're the civilian aide to Secretary Stimson. You handle the... the colored troop issues."

"I handle the Architecture," Gibson corrected gently. He pulled a chair from the corner—FDR's favorite wingback—and sat down without asking. He opened the ledger on his knee. "I keep the books, Harry. And the books are currently unbalanced."

"Robert Weaver," the third man said, introducing himself before Truman could guess. "Economic advisor. I'm here to discuss the rent."

Truman set his glass down. Anger, hot and sudden, began to displace the fear. He was from Missouri. He didn't like being cornered, and he certainly didn't like being talked down to in his own house—especially by people who, by the laws and customs of his state, shouldn't even be using the front door.

"Now you listen here," Truman snapped, standing up and smoothing his jacket. "I don't know what kind of game you people are playing, or where the Secret Service is—and believe me, heads will roll for this—but you are trespassing. The President is dead. The nation is in mourning. I have a war to win. I don't have time for... for grievances. If you want to petition for civil rights, you make an appointment like everyone else."

Gibson looked at Weaver. Weaver looked at Bethune. A silent communication passed between them—a look of exhausted patience, like parents dealing with a tantruming toddler.

"Grievances?" Gibson chuckled softly. "Harry, we didn't come here to ask for anything. We came to give you the keys."

"The keys?"

"To the machine," Gibson said. "You think you inherited a government? You inherited a facade. A storefront. The real business... the business that won the Depression and is currently winning the War... that happens in the back room. And we have been running the back room since 1921."

**III. THE REVELATION**

Bethune stepped forward. She tapped the cane on the floor. *Tap. Tap.*

"Do you know why Franklin couldn't walk, Harry?" she asked.

"Polio," Truman said, confused. "Infantile paralysis. Campobello, 1921. Everyone knows that. Proof of his indomitable spirit. He overcame..."

"He didn't overcome anything," Bethune said. "He was humbled. He was broken. And we did it."

Truman stared at her. "You... you did what?"

"Franklin was a difficult young man," she spoke as if discussing a wayward student. "An arrogant patrician. He thought the world belonged to him by right of blood. He had no empathy. He had no vision beyond his own ambition. We saw potential in him—a vessel, a charmer, a voice—but he needed a rudder. He needed to understand suffering. He needed to understand dependance."

She gestured to the medical bag.

"The virus wasn't wild, Harry. It was cultured. A specific strain of enterovirus we developed in the laboratories at Tuskegee, before the funding was... oficial. We introduced it into the water supply at Campobello. Just for him. It was a surgical strike. We took his legs. We took his autonomy. And in exchange, we offered him a life."

"You're insane," Truman whispered. "This is treason. This is... this is murder."

"It was a negotiation," Gibson interjected, running a finger down a column of numbers. "We offered him a deal. We had the treatment. Not a cure—the nerves were gone—but a serum. The Serum of the Golden Mean. A nutritional and enzymatic protocol derived from the work of Dr. Carver. It managed the pain. It cleared the fog of fatigue. It gave him that boundless, radiant energy—that 'Happy Warrior' smile that terrified his enemies and comforted the nation. Without it, he would have been a bedridden invalid in constant agony. With it, he was the Titan."

"And the price?" Truman asked, horrified fascination taking hold.

"Access," Weaver said simply. "Infrastructure. We didn't want money. We wanted the alphabet agencies. The WPA. The NYA. The CCC. We wanted the federal mechanisms to build a state within a state. While Franklin was giving fireside chats, we were using the Federal Writers' Project to document our history. We were using the PWA to build our schools. We were using the war industry to force integration on the factory floor. Executive Order 8802 wasn't his idea, Harry. It was the rent payment for that month's supply of serum."

Truman sank back onto the sofa. It made a terrible, twisted kind of sense. The "Black Cabinet." The unofficial advisors who were always there, lurking in the background, surviving scandal after scandal. The way FDR seemed to have boundless energy right up until...

"Until he didn't," Truman realized. He looked at Bethune. "What happened? Why did he die?"

Bethune’s face softened, but it was a sad, terrible softness. "He stopped paying the rent, Harry."

**IV. THE BREACH**

She walked over to the sofa and sat down next to him. She smelled of lavender and antiseptic.

"He began to believe the myth," she said quietly. "He forgot that he was the vessel. He started thinking he was the source. Yalta... Yalta changed him. He wanted to secure his legacy as the great peacemaker."

"He tried to fire us," Gibson said from the chair. "He tried to evict the landlords. He planned to sideline the Black Cabinet after the war because he thought he didn't need the Serum anymore."

"He made a mistake," Bethune continued, her voice hardening. "He was about to guarantee that all black soldiers would receive the same treatment as whites under the GI Bill."

Truman frowned. "And that's... bad? Isn't that what you want? Uplift?"

"Not yet," Weaver cut in, sharp and fast. "We cannot have a people rising up who do not owe their rise to *us*. We are the gatekeepers, Harry. We decide when they rise. And it is not yet time."

"He capitulated," Bethune said, "Eventually, he agreed to take blacks out of the bill. But he was becoming difficult to control. Unpredictable."

"You will not be difficult, will you, Harry?" Gibson said, his voice low and dangerous. "You will not go off script."

"Gibson, you are scaring him," Bethune said. "This is a lot for him to take in. Harry, we did not want to stop the shipments, but he forced our hand. We had no other choice."

"You killed him," Truman whispered.

Bethune reached into her medical bag and pulled out a small, amber vial. It was empty. "We handed control back to nature. That wasted, skeletal look he had in the photos from Yalta? That was not merely stress. That was cellular collapse. His body was trying to operate at full capacity without the fuel. He burned himself out from the inside. The hemorrhage at Warm Springs... that was the lights going out."

"I was there," she added, her voice a whisper. "I went down to Georgia. Not to save him. But to say goodbye. I held his hand, Harry. Not to comfort him, but to time the pulse. We loved him, in a way. But you do not keep a broken tool."

The silence in the room was deafening. The ticking of the clocks seemed to have stopped. Truman felt a cold sweat trickling down his back.

"Are you..." He swallowed hard. "Are you going to kill me?"

Gibson laughed. It was a dry, startling sound. "Kill you? Harry, look at you. You are healthy as a horse. You walk five miles every morning. You have a strong heart. You don not need any medicine."

He snapped the ledger shut.

"But you need the *Brain*."

**V. THE PROPOSITION**

Gibson stood up and paced the room. "You are terrified. You feel small. You know that you are not equipped for this. You do not have the connections. You do not have the intelligence network. You do not have the love of the people. You are the 'Senator from Pendergast.' A machine politician from Missouri."

"I am the President!" Truman shouted, but there was no heat in it.

"You are a man standing in the shadow of a god," Weaver corrected. "And the shadow is going to swallow you. Unless we turn on the lights."

Gibson leaned over the back of the sofa, bringing his face close to Truman's.

"We have the network, Harry. The Sleeping Car Porters. The press. The artists. The scientists. We have eyes in every factory, ears in every kitchen. We have the Manhattan Project—oh yes, we know about the gadget. Dr. Carver's disciples are running the calculations on the implosion lens right now."

"We can make you great," Bethune said softly. "We can stabilize your transition. We can give you the victories you need to look like a leader. We can deliver the labor unions. We can deliver the urban vote. We can make sure the Transition is seamless."

"And in exchange?" Truman asked. He was trembling. He hated them, but he needed them. God help him, he needed someone to tell him what to do.

"The Missouri Compromise," Gibson said. "A new one."

"Desegregation," Truman guessed.

"Total," Gibson said. "The Military. Immediately. No 'all deliberate speed.' You will issue the Executive Order. You will integrate the Armed Forces. You will integrate the federal hiring practices. You will appoint a Civil Rights Commission with teeth."

"The Generals will revolt," Truman gasped. "Marshall... Eisenhower..."

"Let them," Weaver said. "We will give you the strategy to win the war so quickly they won't have time to complain. And that brings us to the second condition."

Gibson walked to the window and looked out at the rain.

"The Gadget. The Bomb. You're going to use it."

Truman blinked. "Secretary Stimson said... said it was a last resort. To invade Japan would cost a million lives..."

"You will use it," Gibson said, turning around. His eyes were hard. "Not to save lives. But to demonstrate Power. Absolute, technological supremacy. The world needs to see that the old ways of war are over. The sheer, blinding light of that weapon... it is the only thing that will reset the board. It will end the war instantly, and it will give you the political capital to do what we need at home."

"But the civilians..."

"Are collateral," Gibson said coldly. "We are talking about a new world order, Harry. A world where science rules over sentiment. We need the shock. We need the awe. Drop the bomb. End the war. Bring the boys home. And then... integrate them."

**VI. THE OATH**

Truman looked at the three of them. The Matriarch, the Architect, the Economist. They weren't asking. They were informing.

He looked at the empty glass of bourbon. He looked at the rain streaking the window. He thought about the crushing weight of the next four years. He thought about the mockery in the papers. *The Little Man from Missouri.*

If he said no... what? Would he trip on the stairs? Would his heart stop in his sleep? Or would he just fail? Would he flail and drown in the complexities of the office, a mediocrity exposed to history?

He didn't want to be a mediocrity. He wanted to be the President.

He looked at Mary McLeod Bethune. She held out the cane. The blackthorn stick. The scepter of the dead king.

"Walk with us, Harry," she said. "And you will never stumble. Walk alone, and you will crawl."

Truman reached out. His hand shook, but he took the cane. The wood was warm, smooth from years of FDR's grip. It felt heavy. It felt like power.

He realized then that the silence in the hallway wasn't just an absence of guards. It was a transfer of allegiance. The Secret Service knew. The household staff knew. The real power had arrived, and the guards had stepped aside to let it in.

He gripped the handle of the cane. He felt a strange surge of confidence. Maybe it was the bourbon. Maybe it was the wood. Or maybe it was simply the relief of knowing that he didn't have to carry the weight alone.

He looked up at Truman Gibson. He sat up straighter. He adjusted his glasses.

"Draft the Order," Truman said. "For the military. I want it on my desk by morning."

Gibson smiled. It was the first genuine smile of the night. He opened the ledger and uncapped a fountain pen.

"It's already written, Mr. President," Gibson said. "Sign here."

Truman took the pen. He didn't look at the text. He signed his name.

*Harry S. Truman.*

The ink was black. It looked like oil. It looked like blood.

"Good," Bethune said. She stood up and smoothed her coat. "We will let you rest now, Harry. You have a big day tomorrow. You have a war to win."

They turned to leave. They didn't bow. They didn't ask for permission to depart. They just walked out, the sound of their footsteps fading down the hall.

*Tap. Tap. Tap.*

Truman sat alone in the Yellow Oval Room. He held the cane across his lap like a sword. He looked at the clock on the mantel.

It started ticking again.

*Minutes recorded by Truman Gibson, The Architect.*
