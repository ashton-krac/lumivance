# CHAPTER 13: THE PSYCHOLOGICAL PROFILE

**DATE:** April 15, 1928  
**LOCATION:** The Library of the 135th Street Branch, New York Public Library (The Schomburg Center)  
**TIME:** 10:00 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Grand Architect Nella Larsen (The Novelist/Librarian)  

**ATTENDEES (THE HARLEM RENAISSANCE VETTING COMMITTEE):**  
1.  **Nella Larsen** - *Grand Architect*  
2.  **Carl Van Vechten** (White Patron) - *The Keymaster*  
3.  **Jessie Fauset** (Editor of *The Crisis*) - *The Gatekeeper*  
4.  **Alain Locke** (Rhodes Scholar) - *The Philosopher*  
5.  **Zora Neale Hurston** (Anthropologist) - *The Wildcard*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER**

"The reviews are in," Jessie Fauset said. "The whites love it. They think Helga Crane is a symbol of the 'marginal man,' torn between two worlds, crushed by her own neuroses."

The library was closed to the public. The "Schomburg Collection" of black history was being cataloged. But tonight, the committee was cataloging souls.

Nella Larsen sat at the head table. Her novel, *Quicksand*, was just published. The critics called it a masterpiece of the "tragic mulatto" genre. Nella knew it was actually a personality test.

"Good," Nella said. "Let them pity her. Now, have we distributed the copies to the target list?"

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE 'QUICKSAND' LITMUS TEST**

"We sent copies to the top students at Howard, Fisk, and Atlanta University," Alain Locke reported. "Along with a 'reader survey' asking for their interpretation of Helga's dilemma."

"And the results?" Nella asked.

"We have categorized them," Locke said. "80% of the respondents wrote essays about how 'sad' Helga was. They identified with her confusion. They felt her pain."

"Reject them," Nella ordered. "They are weak. They have internalized the pathology. They believe the lie that mixing blood creates madness."

"15% of the respondents," Locke continued, "criticized Helga. They said she was selfish, impulsive, and should have settled down."

"Reject them too," Nella said. "They are bourgeois. They want comfort, not power. They will betray us for a nice house."

"And the remaining 5%?"

"The 5%," Locke smiled. "They wrote back saying Helga was a fool not because she was mixed, but because she didn't *use* her ambiguity. One student wrote: 'Helga failed because she tried to belong to a race, instead of ruling both.' Another wrote: 'Her tragedy was that she had no mission.'"

"Those are the ones," Nella said, her eyes gleaming. "Those are the Lumis. Find them. Recruit them."

**III. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE VAN VECHTEN PROTOCOL**

"What about the whites?" Zora Neale Hurston asked, playing with her hat. "Are we letting Carl here invite all of downtown to our parties?"

Carl Van Vechten, the white writer who was obsessed with Harlem, grinned. "My parties are the perfect filter, Zora. I invite the wildest, richest white folks. And I invite your 'Talented Tenth.' We get them drunk. We play jazz. And we watch."

**IV. SIDEBAR: THE VAN VECHTEN PARTIES**

"It is a laboratory," Nella explained. "When you put a young black poet in a room with a Vanderbilt heiress who has had three gin rickeys, you see his true character."

"Most of them crumble," Carl noted, lighting a cigarette. "They become eager to please. They start tapping their feet. They start telling stories about their 'mammy' down south. They prostitute their trauma for a smile."

"We call those 'The Entertainers'," Locke said with disdain. "They are useful for distraction, but they can never be trusted with power. They are addicted to white approval."

"But then," Carl said, his voice dropping, "you see the others. The ones who stand still. The ones who let the heiress come to them. The ones who accept the drink but do not say thank you. The ones who look at the white wealth not with envy, but with appraisal. Like a butcher eyeing a cow."

"Those are the 'Handlers'," Nella said. "We mark them. We invite them to the inner circle. Because if you can handle a drunken white woman without losing your dignity, you can handle a negotiation with a senator."

"It is a dangerous game," Jessie Fauset warned. "The papers call it 'mongrelization.' They say we are corrupting the youth."

"We *are* corrupting them," Nella agreed. "We are corrupting their innocence. We are corrupting their belief in white superiority. By the time they leave Carl's apartment at 4 a.m., they know the secret: The white man is not a god. He is just a drunk with a checkbook."

**IV-A. SIDEBAR: THE WHITE PATRON'S DIARY**

Carl laughed, tapping the pocket of his tweed jacket. "I write it all down, strictly for anthropological purposes, of course. Just last night, I observed a fascinating interaction between young Countee Cullen and the daughter of a Standard Oil executive."

"Do tell," Zora leaned in.

"She was lecturing him on the 'spiritual purity' of the Negro soul," Carl recounted, mimicking the breathless accent of a debutante. "She told him that his poetry was 'too structured' and that he should write more like a 'child of nature.' And Countee... bless him... he didn't flinch. He just looked at her over the rim of his champagne glass and asked, 'And does a child of nature understand the sonnet form better than Keats?'"

"And her reaction?" Locke asked.

"She wept," Carl grinned. "She was devastated. She wanted him to be a savage, and he quoted Keats. It broke her heart. And then, feeling guilty for her racism, she wrote him a check for five hundred dollars to 'nurture his natural gift.' It was a masterclass in manipulation."

**V. SIDEBAR: THE TOWER ARCHIVES**

"Speaking of secrets," A'Lelia Walker said, entering the room. She was the hostess of 'The Dark Tower,' the most exclusive salon in Harlem. She was tall, regal, and terrifying. She carried a leather satchel.

"The harvest from last night," she said, dropping the satchel on the table.

Loche opened it. Inside were photographs. Grainy, candid shots of white Wall Street bankers in compromising positions with black jazz musicians. Senators inhaling opium. Debate club presidents from Yale dancing the Charleston with chorus girls.

**V-A. SIDEBAR: THE BLACKMAIL LEDGER**

"File 88," A'Lelia pointed to a photo of a man passed out on a velvet sofa. "That is the Chairman of the Housing Committee. He voted against the anti-lynching bill. He also oddly enjoys dressing up as a French maid."

"Useful," Nella noted, marking the photo with a red pen.

"File 92," A'Lelia continued. "The son of the Police Commissioner. He comes to the Tower every Tuesday looking for 'integrity.' We give him gin. He gives us the patrol schedules for the speakeasies."

"The 'Dark Tower' is not just a party," A'Lelia said. "It is a trap. We specifically designed the lighting to encourage intimacy. We specifically hired bartenders who know how to mix drinks that lower inhibitions but keep the memory intact. And we specifically placed cameras behind the two-way mirrors in the powder rooms."

"Blackmail?" Jessie Fauset asked, clutching her pearls. "This is low, A'Lelia."

"This is insurance," A'Lelia corrected. "Do you think the Renaissance is funded by book sales? *Quicksand* sold three thousand copies. That barely pays for the paper. The movement is funded by silence. We have the files on three board members of the Chase Bank. We have the files on the Deputy Mayor. As long as they want these photos to stay in the Tower, they will keep signing the grants for the Art Centers."

"It is the 'Voodoo of Finance' again," Zora laughed. "We are selling them protection from their own shadows."

"And when the market crashes?" Carl asked suddenly. "Because it will. My friends on Wall Street are nervous. The bubble is too big."

"That," Nella said, "is why we need the Niggerati."

**VI. SIDEBAR: THE NIGGERATI MANIFESTO**

The door opened without a knock. Wallace Thurman stood there, pale as a ghost, holding a gin bottle. He was the darkest member of the Renaissance, but his skin was so light he passed for white in the Village. He was the walking embodiment of the ambiguity Nella wrote about.

"Did someone call for the court jester?" Thurman slurred, dropping into a chair.

"We were discussing your manifesto, Wallace," Locke said. "Or rather, the *Fire!!* magazine."

"Fire," Thurman laughed bitterly. "We burned it all down, didn't we? We told the truth. We wrote about prostitutes, about homosexuals, about colorism within the race. And what happened? The black church hated us. The white liberals loved us. We became a zoo exhibit."

He took a swig of gin.

"The 'New Negro' is a fraud, Alain. He is a invention of your imagination. He doesn't exist. The real Negro is just trying to pay rent. But you... you have turned us into a commodity. You sell our pain to the highest bidder."

"We sell *access*," Nella corrected. "We sell the *idea* of the Negro. Because ideas are weapons. You think your cynicism is rebellion, Wallace. It isn't. It is just another flavor of entertainment for them. They love a cynical Negro. It makes them feel sophisticated."

Thurman stared at her. "You are colder than ice, Nella."

"Ice preserves," Nella said. "Fire consumes. The Renaissance is burning out, Wallace. We need to preserve what matters."

**VII. SIDEBAR: THE MARKET CORRECTION**

"She is right," a deep voice said from the shadows. Caspar Holstein stepped forward. The Bolito King. The man who funded the *Opportunity* prizes with illegal lottery money.

"I track the money," Holstein said. "The white money. The Van Vechten money. The Guggenheim money. It is drying up."

"Why?" Zora asked.

"Because the market is overheating," Holstein explained. "I see the ledger books of the men who play my numbers. They are leveraged to the hilt. They are buying stocks on margin. When the correction comes—and it will come within a year—they will not care about Negro art. They will not care about the 'New Negro.' They will be too busy jumping out of windows."

"What does that mean for us?" Fauset asked.

"It means the party is over," Holstein said flatly. "The age of the 'Artist' is ending. The age of the 'Economic Nationalist' must begin. We must stop relying on their patronage. We must build our own banks. Our own stores. Our own armies. Because when the crash comes, they will not feed us. They will eat us."

**VIII. RESOLUTION: THE PIVOT**

"So we pivot," Nella declared. "We stop recruiting poets. We start recruiting economists."

"And the poets we have?" Locke asked.

"We weaponize them," Nella said. "Or we discard them. The mission has changed."

"It seems cruel," Zora whispered.

"It is evolution," Nella said. "Survival of the fittest."

**IX. SIDEBAR: THE CENSUS OF SHADOWS**

"We are tracking more than just debt," A'Lelia interjected, opening her ledger again. "We are tracking movement. The 'Census of Shadows.'"

She laid out a map of the United States. It was covered in black lines.

"Each line represents a family unit of the Talented Tenth that we have moved in the last six months," she explained. "We are emptying the South. We are draining the intellectual capital of the Confederacy and depositing it in the swing states of the North. Ohio. Pennsylvania. Michigan. By 1932, we will have enough votes in key districts to swing the presidential election."

"Gerrymandering by migration," Carl marveled. "You are literally moving the population to suit your political needs."

"We are terraforming the electorate," A'Lelia corrected. "And we are doing it quietly. One family at a time. The white man thinks it is just 'labor looking for work.' He does not see the strategic deployment."

**X. SIDEBAR: THE EUROPEAN EXIT**

"And the capital?" Locke asked. "If the crash is coming, where do we put the money?"

"Paris," Nella said instantly. "And Berlin. We are buying real estate in Europe while the dollar is still strong. When the American market collapses, we will be holding hard assets in foreign currencies. We will be the only ones with liquidity."

"It is a bet against America," Carl whispered.

"It is a hedge," Nella said. "America is a volatility engine. It booms and busts. We cannot afford to ride the rollercoaster. We need stability. And right now, oddly enough, the most stable place for a Negro's money is in the chaotic markets of Weimar Germany. They are so desperate for currency they don't care whose face is on the bill."

**XI. SIDEBAR: THE LONDON PROTOCOL**

"I received a cable from London," Locke mentioned, changing the subject. "Robeson is making waves. *Show Boat* is a hit. But he is also meeting with the Labour Party."

"I received a cable from London," Locke mentioned, changing the subject. "Robeson is making waves. *Show Boat* is a hit. But he is also meeting with the Labour Party."

"Excellent," Nella smiled. "Paul is the perfect ambassador. He has the voice of a god and the mind of a Marxist. But tell him to be careful. The British Intelligence service is watching him."

"He knows," Locke said. "He is using the 'London Protocol.' He speaks in parables. When he sings 'Ol' Man River,' he is not singing about the Mississippi. He is singing about the inevitable flow of revolution. The white audience cries because they think it is sad. He cries because he knows it is a threat."

"That is the difference," Nella noted. "Between art and propaganda. Art makes you feel. Propaganda makes you act. We need both."

**X. SIDEBAR: THE ANTHROPOLOGY OF DEBT**

"And back home?" Caspar Holstein asked. "What is the mood in the South, Zora?"

"Hungry," Zora replied, her voice losing its usual playfulness. "The sharecroppers are starving. The boll weevil took the cotton. The flood took the houses. Now the company store takes the rest."

"Debt," Holstein nodded. "It is the new chain."

"But also a weapon," Zora added. "I found a community in the Florida swamps who have stopped using currency. They trade in labor. They trade in secrets. They have effectively seceded from the American economy. If we can replicate that model—if we can create a 'Shadow Economy' of debt forgiveness within the race—we can break the power of the white banks."

"A jubilee," Locke whispered. "Biblical."

"Practical," Zora corrected. "If 12 million Negroes refuse to pay their debts on the same day, the American banking system collapses. It is not just a strike. It is financial assassination."

**XI. SIDEBAR: THE AESTHETIC OF COOL**

"There is one more requirement," Locke said, polishing his glasses. "We must teach them how to be 'Cool.'"

"There is one more requirement," Locke said, polishing his glasses. "We must teach them how to be 'Cool.'"

"Cool?" Zora asked. "Like temperature?"

"Like armor," Locke said. "What is the defining trait of the American Negro experience? It is the requirement to react. To react to insults. To react to violence. To react to kindness. We are always reacting. 'Cool' is the refusal to react. It is the suspension of emotion. It is the jazz musician turning his back to the audience to tune his instrument. It sends a message: 'I am here, but I am not *for* you.'"

"It is power," Nella agreed. "If they cannot make you angry, and they cannot make you grateful, they do not know what to do with you. You become a variable they cannot solve."

"It is a mask," Locke continued. "But unlike the minstrel mask, which was made of burnt cork and exaggerated emotion, this mask is made of ice. It is impenetrable. And behind it, we are plotting."

**XII. SIDEBAR: THE PSYCHOLOGY OF PASSING**

"And for those who can solve the variable?" Zora asked. "Those who can cross the line?"

"The passers," Nella nodded. "My favorite subjects. In *Quicksand*, Helga cannot decide. But in my next book, *Passing*, I will reveal the truth. Passing is not betrayal. It is infiltration."

She stood up and walked to the window, looking out at the Harlem night.

"We have three hundred operatives currently living as white men and women in New York City. They are married to bankers. They are working in the State Department. They are raising blonde children. They are the ultimate sleeper agents. They hear what the white man says when he thinks no one is listening. And every Tuesday, they send a report to the Dark Tower."

"Do they ever want to come back?" Carl asked.

"Always," Nella said. "The strain is unbearable. To deny one's blood is to deny one's soul. That is why they are the bravest of us all. They are sacrificing their identity for the intelligence."

**XI. THE OATH OF THE PAGE**

Nella picked up a copy of *Quicksand*. She signed the inside cover.

"To the 5%," she wrote. "To those who do not sink."

She handed it to Locke. "Send this to the student who wrote about ruling both races. I want him in New York by Monday."

**XII. SIDEBAR: THE FINAL TOAST**

"The gin is gone," Zora noted, tilting the bottle.

"Good," Nella said. "The Renaissance was a gin party. Fast. intoxicating. Cheap. The next era... the next era will be whiskey. Dark. Slow. Calculated."

She pulled a bottle of aged bourbon from her desk drawer. She poured five glasses.

"To the end of the party," she proposed.

"To the hangover," Wallace Thurman countered, raising his glass.

"To the work," Locke said quietly.

They drank. The bourbon burned, a necessary cauterization. The music from the street below was changing. The frenetic Charleston was fading. A slower, deeper blues was rising from the pavement. The depression was coming, but the Lumivance was already digging the storm cellar.

**XIII. ADJOURNMENT**

The meeting ended. Zora went off to collect folklore (and codes) in the South. Carl went to find more gin. Nella Larsen remained in the library, surrounded by books, guarding the secret that her "fiction" was actually the operating manual for the Third Race.

She closed the book. The decade was over. The 1920s had been a party. The 1930s would be a war. Nella Larsen was ready.

The library was silent. The books were waiting. The war had begun.
