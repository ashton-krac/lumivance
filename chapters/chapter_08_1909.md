# CHAPTER 8: THE FRONT

**DATE:** February 12, 1909 (The Centenary of Abraham Lincoln’s Birth)  
**LOCATION:** The Apartment of Mary White Ovington, A "Socialist Tenement," New York City  
**TIME:** 8:00 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Grand Architect W.E.B. Du Bois  

**ATTENDEES (THE FOUNDING COMMITTEE OF THE NAACP):**  
1. **W.E.B. Du Bois** - *Grand Architect*  
2. **Mary White Ovington** (White Socialist) - *The Useful Idiot (Treasurer)*  
3. **Oswald Garrison Villard** (Grandson of William Lloyd Garrison) - *The Legacy Hire*  
4. **Ida B. Wells** (The Firebrand) - *The Enforcer (Skeptical)*  
5. **William English Walling** (Wealthy Southerner) - *The Wallet*  
6. **Henry Moskowitz** (Jewish Activist) - *The Bridge*  
7. **Silas** (The Waiter) - *The Silent Witness*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE SMOKE OF RADICALISM**

"We must strike while the iron is hot," Mary White Ovington declared, her eyes shining with fervor as she paced the room, a cigarette holder clamped in her teeth. "The Springfield Riot was a pogrom. Lincoln's hometown! White mobs burning Negro homes within sight of the Great Emancipator's grave. It proves that the North is no better than the South. We need a permanent organization to fight this."

The apartment on West 38th Street was a "Socialist Tenement," a deliberate affectation of poverty by Ovington, a woman born into comfortable wealth who had chosen to live among the working poor. The air was thick with the smoke of Turkish cigarettes and the fog of self-righteousness.

The room was filled with the earnest energy of white liberals—"New Abolitionists" who wore their guilt like a badge of honor. They spoke loudly of "free love" and "the proletariat," though their clothes were still tailored and their trust funds intact.

W.E.B. Du Bois sat at the head of the crowded table, sipping peppermint tea. He was the only Black man in the room besides a silent waiter named Silas. Du Bois was impeccable, his demeanor aristocratic. to him, these people were not allies; they were camouflage. He watched them pontificate about "struggle" while Silas silently refilled their crystal glasses.

"Agreed," Oswald Garrison Villard nodded. He was the grandson of William Lloyd Garrison, the great abolitionist, and he carried the weight of that legacy like a heavy cross. He felt a personal responsibility for the failure of Reconstruction. "My grandfather would be ashamed of this country. We must revive the spirit of the Anti-Slavery Society. We shall call it the 'National Negro Committee.' Or perhaps the 'League for Civil Rights.'"

"No," Du Bois said softly, his voice cutting through the chatter like a razor. It was a quiet voice, but it had the gravity of a judge passing sentence.

The room fell silent. When the Professor spoke, the children listened.

"The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People," Du Bois said.

"A bit... wordy, isn't it?" William English Walling noted. He was a wealthy Southerner who had turned traitor to his class, scandalizing his Kentucky family by marrying a Russian socialist. He still spoke with a drawl that made some in the room wince.

"It is precise," Du Bois countered. "Advancement implies evolution. It implies upward movement. It is a scientific term, Oswald. And we are dealing with biology, not just politics. We are not just asking for rights; we are asserting our capacity to *advance*. It frames the argument not as a plea for mercy, but as a recognition of potential."

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE FUNDING STRUCTURE (THE BRIDGE OF GUILT)**

"We have pledges," Walling said, opening a leather ledger. "My family fortune is at your disposal. I cannot undo the sins of my ancestors—my grandfather owned two hundred slaves—but I can pay for their correction. Mr. Villard has pledged the support of the *New York Evening Post*. We have the capital and the press. We have the 'Garrison' name."

"Excellent," Du Bois said. "I propose that the executive board be... diverse. We need white faces at the top. We need a white President. We need a white Treasurer."

"That is very humble of you, Dr. Du Bois," Ovington smiled, clearly touched. "Most Negro leaders—like Mr. Washington—demand total control. They treat their organizations like personal fiefdoms."

"I am not interested in titles," Du Bois said smoothly. "I am interested in results. A white President legitimizes us. It makes the government less likely to raid our offices. It makes the Southern sheriff pause before he burns our pamphlets. You are the shield, Mary. You are the armor. When the Klan looks at the NAACP, they must see white faces protecting the black ones."

Ida B. Wells, sitting in the corner, scoffed audibly. She was sipping whiskey, not tea. She knew exactly what Du Bois was doing. He was building a front. He was creating a "White Shield" that would allow the Order to operate in plain sight. If the President was white, the organization couldn't be a "radical Negro plot." It was just "philanthropy."

"I will take the position of Director of Publicity and Research," Du Bois said. "I will edit the magazine. *The Crisis*."

"A fine name," Villard agreed. "Urgent. Modern. It captures the moment."

"The board will handle the lawsuits," Du Bois continued, assigning roles like a general. "Mr. Villard, Mr. Moskowitz—you will fight the legal battles. You will go to the Supreme Court. You will argue for the Constitution. You will use the white man's law against him. I... I will handle the *soul* of the organization. I will talk to the people."

"But surely the legal battle is the most important?" Henry Moskowitz asked. He was a Jewish activist who saw parallels between the pogroms of Russia and the lynchings of the South.

"To you, perhaps," Du Bois said. "But a people without a voice are merely a client state. *The Crisis* will be the voice. It will define what it means to be Black in the 20th Century."

**III. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE TROJAN HORSE STRATEGY**

Du Bois reached into his briefcase and pulled out a mocked-up cover of the first issue of *The Crisis*. It did not feature a lynching victim or a protest march. It featured a pencil drawing of a young colored girl, beautiful and serene, looking up at the sky.

"This is propaganda?" Walling asked, confused. "It looks like an art journal. It looks... soft."

"We will use your money," Du Bois told the white members, "to print a hundred thousand copies of this continually. We will send it to every barbershop, every church, every parlor in Black America. We will subsidize the subscription so that even the sharecropper can afford it. You think we are printing news. We are not. We are printing *code*."

"Code?" Ovington asked, tilting her head. "Like... secret messages? I loved those as a child. My father used to hide riddles in the garden."

"Cultural code, Mary," Du Bois corrected quickly, covering his slip with a charming smile that didn't quite reach his eyes. "We are teaching them how to be citizens. How to vote. How to dress. How to hold their heads high. We are teaching them 'Double Consciousness'—the ability to see themselves through their own eyes, not just the eyes of the white world. It is a psychological vaccination against inferiority."

Villard picked up the mock-up. "Wait," he said, squinting at a poem in the corner. "This poem... 'The Song of the Smoke.' It seems to be referencing... fire? 'I am the smoke king, I am black.' It sounds almost... militant."

Du Bois felt a spike of adrenaline. Villard was smarter than he looked.

"It is a metaphor, Oswald," Du Bois said smoothly. "The smoke of industry. The Negro as the fuel of the American engine. It is a celebration of labor."

Villard studied it for a moment longer, then nodded. "Ah. Yes. Of course. Very poetic."

Du Bois exhaled internally. *Too close.*

*In reality,* Du Bois thought, catching Ida's eye, *we are teaching them the hierarchy.* *The Crisis* would be the primary recruitment tool for the Order. The "Talented Tenth" essays he planned to write were not philosophical musings; they were job descriptions. He would use the magazine to identify the brightest minds, the "Lumi" candidates.

"For example," Du Bois continued, pointing to a proposed article titled 'The Problem of Amusement.' "It looks like a moralizing piece about dance halls. But read between the lines. I am telling our young men where to gather. I am telling them which establishments are safe, which are owned by the Order, and which are traps set by the police. I am giving them a map of the city, disguised as a sermon."

He flipped to another page.

"And here. 'Men of the Month.' We profile successful Negro doctors, lawyers, architects. To you, it is uplift. To me, it is a directory. I am signaling to our cells who has resources, who can be tapped for funds, who can provide shelter. I am building a national network, Mary, and you are paying for the printing press."

The "Children's Number" of *The Crisis*, ostensibly a celebration of black youth, would actually be a catalog of the next generation of agents. Photographs of "Beautiful colored babies" submitted by proud parents would allow the Order to track phenotypes and bloodlines across the country. He would build a database of the race, funded by white philanthropy.

Silas, the waiter, refilled Du Bois's tea. As he did so, he tapped the table twice with his index finger—a signal that he had successfully intercepted Walling's coat check ticket, which contained a note he needed to read. Du Bois nodded imperceptibly. Even the help was part of the machine.

**IV. THE WHITE ALLIES: USEFUL IDIOTS**

Du Bois looked at Ovington. She was a good woman. She truly believed in equality. She would die for the cause. And that made her dangerous, because she assumed everyone played by the same rules of honor.

"Mary," Du Bois said gently. "You must understand that you will be hated. 'Nigger-lover.' 'Traitor.' These are the names they will call you. You will lose friends. You will be uninvited from the balls. The police will open your mail."

"I do not care about society," Ovington said fiercely. She recounted a story from her youth, seeing a black man beaten in the street while polite society walked by. "I care about justice. I care about the fact that my comfort is built on their suffering."

"Good," Du Bois said. "Because we will need your anger. But we also need your *access*. You can walk into rooms we cannot. You can dine with Senators. You can whisper in the ears of bankers. You are our spy in the house of the enemy."

"I am not a spy, William," she protested. "I am a friend."

"Of course," Du Bois soothed. "A friend." *A friend who opens the door just wide enough for us to slip in the knife.*

**V. RESOLUTION: THE EXCLUSION OF BOOKER T.**

"One condition," Ida B. Wells spoke up, her voice raspy from the tobacco smoke. "Washington cannot be involved. If the Wizard of Tuskegee gets his hands on this, he will turn it into a trade school brochure. He will tell the Negroes to 'cast down their buckets' and be happy with their lot."

"Now, wait," Walling hesitated. "Washington has power. He has the ear of Roosevelt. He raises millions. Alienating him might be... imprudent."

"Washington represents the 19th Century," Du Bois said coldly. "He represents the servant class. The NAACP must represent the thinking class. If we invite him, he will dominate. He will tell the donors that we are too radical. He will tell you that the Negro is not ready for the vote."

"He calls us 'The Radicals,'" Ovington noted.

"We *are* radicals," Du Bois said. "Because we believe a black man has a brain as well as a back. By excluding Washington, we define ourselves. We are the anti-Tuskegee. We are the vanguard. The NAACP is the head; Tuskegee is the hands. And the head does not ask the hands for permission to think."

"We shall draft a resolution excluding Mr. Washington from the steering committee," Villard decided. "Regrettably, of course. We will say his schedule is too full to burden him with this new enterprise."

**VI. THE OATH OF THE FRONT**

"Then it is settled," Ovington beamed, raising her glass. "We are the NAACP. Black and White, working together for the first time in history on equal footing. It is a new day."

Du Bois raised his teacup. "To cooperation."

"To cooperation!" the whites echoed.

Ida B. Wells caught Du Bois's eye. He winked—a micro-gesture, barely visible. She understood. The whites were the engine; they provided the fuel (money) and the vehicle (legal standing). But the Order was the steering wheel. They would drive this bus exactly where they wanted it to go, and the passengers would think they were just along for the ride.

**VII. SIDEBAR: THE ENFORCER**

As the meeting broke up and the whites busied themselves with signing the "Call" document—a parchment that Walling had commissioned from a calligrapher for this exact purpose—Ida pulled Du Bois onto the fire escape. The cold February air hit them like a slap, a welcome relief from the suffocating warmth of Ovington's parlor.

Below them, the city of New York sprawled out, a grid of electric lights and dark alleys. It was a machine, huming with the energy of commerce and corruption.

"You are a devil, William," Ida said, lighting a cigar with a friction match she struck against the brick wall. She inhaled deeply, the tip glowing red in the darkness. "You are using them. And you are enjoying it."

"I am using their *resources*, Ida," Du Bois said, looking out at the city lights. He adjusted his silk scarf. "They want to be saviors. It is a psychological need for them—a way to absolve themselves of the guilt of their inheritance without actually giving up their trust funds. Who am I to deny them their salvation? If their money buys the legal precedents that protect our cells, if their names shield our operatives... then let them feel noble."

"They think they are leading," Ida laughed darkly, exhaling a plume of smoke. "Poor Mary thinks she is the Joan of Arc of the Negro. She thinks she is guiding us out of the wilderness."

"Let her think it," Du Bois said. "The best puppet master is the one who makes the puppet believe it is dancing on its own. We are building a Trojan Horse, Ida. And tonight, we just painted it white. Inside *The Crisis*, I will hide the revolution. Between the lines of every article, I will whisper to the Talented Tenth. And Mary White Ovington will pay for the postage."

He turned to her, his eyes intense.

"I have already identified three candidates for the inner circle through the preliminary subscription lists," he whispered. "A young Pullman porter in Harlem named Randolph. A lawyer in D.C. And a chemistry student in Alabama who has figured out how to make explosives from fertilizer. We will recruit them next month."

Ida looked at him, impressed despite herself. "You move fast, Professor."

"We must," Du Bois said. "The whites are building skyscrapers. We must build a foundation deep enough to topple them."

**VIII. ADJOURNMENT**

The meeting ended past midnight. The founding of the NAACP was official. To the world, it was the birth of the civil rights movement. To the Order, it was the activation of their public face—a mask of respectability that would hide the grim determination of the Lumi beneath.

Du Bois walked Ida to her carriage.

"1909," he mused. "One hundred years since Lincoln. One hundred years since Darwin. A fitting year for evolution."

"And for revolution?" Ida asked.

"That comes later," Du Bois said, turning up his collar against the wind. "First, we organize. Then, we infiltrate. Then... we rise."
