# CHAPTER 6: THE STRATEGIC LOSS

**DATE:** May 19, 1896  
**LOCATION:** The Private Salon of the Café Richelieu, New Orleans, Louisiana  
**TIME:** 2:00 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Grand Architect Homer Plessy (The Shoemaker)  

**ATTENDEES (THE COMITÉ DES CITOYENS):**  
1. **Homer Plessy** - *Grand Architect (The Plant)*  
2. **Albion Tourgée** (The White Lawyer) - *The Puppet*  
3. **Louis Martinet** (Attorney) - *The Strategist*  
4. **Rodolphe Desdunes** (Historian) - *The Archivist*  
5. **Thomy Lafon** (Spiritually Present via Trust) - *The Financier*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE CHAMPAGNE OF DEFEAT**

"To Justice Henry Billings Brown!" Homer Plessy announced, raising his crystal flute of *Veuve Clicquot 1877*. "To the Supreme Court of the United States! And to the genius of the 'Separate but Equal' doctrine!"

The Café Richelieu was the jewel of French Creole New Orleans, a place where the air was always thick with the scent of roasted coffee, chicory, and the sweet perfume of the magnolia trees. But in the private salon at the back, behind heavy velvet curtains, the mood was electric. It was not the somber atmosphere of a funeral, despite the day's catastrophic news. Instead, it felt like a coronation.

Plessy sat at the head of the table. To the untrained eye, he was a white man—his skin the color of old ivory, his eyes a piercing hazel. He was an "Octoroon," seven-eighths European, a living testament to the absurdity of the color line. Yet, four years ago, he had deliberately walked into the "Whites Only" car of the East Louisiana Railroad, announced his heritage, and waited to be arrested.

He did not look like a man who had just lost a Supreme Court case that would condemn millions of his people to second-class citizenship for the next sixty years. He looked like a man who had just checkmated a king. He scanned the faces of the Comité des Citoyens: Louis Martinet, Rodolphe Desdunes, and the bewildered white attorney, Albion Tourgée.

"Hear, hear!" The committee raised their glasses. Louis Martinet, the brilliant attorney who had orchestrated the legal challenge, grinned like a wolf. Rodolphe Desdunes, the historian who documented their lineage, nodded with solemn satisfaction.

Albion Tourgée, the famous white lawyer and civil rights crusader who had argued the case before the high court, sat frozen in his chair. He looked at the men around him as if they had gone mad. He wiped his brow with a silk handkerchief, his face pale with shock.

"Gentlemen, I... I confess I am baffled by your levity," Tourgée stammered. "We lost. The telegram arrived an hour ago. The Court ruled 7 to 1 against us. Only Justice Harlan dissented. Segregation is now the law of the land. The 'Jim Crow' car is constitutional. It is a catastrophe. We have failed the race."

Louis Martinet placed a comforting hand on Tourgée's shoulder. "Albion, my friend. You are a brilliant legal mind. You have a good heart. But you are a terrible economist. Sit down. Drink your wine. Let us explain to you what we have actually won."

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE MONOPOLY OF EXCLUSION**

Martinet stood up and unrolled a large, detailed map of New Orleans on the table. It showed the city's districts—the French Quarter, the Garden District, Faubourg Marigny. For centuries, New Orleans had been a checkerboard of races, living side by side in a complex, fluid dance. But now, unseen red lines were being drawn across the grid.

"Before today," Martinet explained, using a silver pointer to trace the rail lines, "the Negro dollar was free. It was promiscuous. A colored man of means—a doctor, a lawyer, a skilled artisan—where did he spend his money? He ate at white restaurants, if they would have him. He rode white trains. He bought his suits from white tailors on Canal Street. He insured his house with white companies in New York."

"He gave his wealth to the white man," Plessy said, popping a peeled grape into his mouth. "And the white man took it, even if he sneered while doing so. The white man is a hypocrite, but he is a capitalist first."

"Financial leakage," Desdunes whispered, writing in his ledger. "Economic bleed. Every dollar a black man spends in a white establishment is a dollar lost to the Order. It is wealth that leaves our community and never returns. It builds their mansions, paves their streets, educates their children."

"But now," Martinet continued, his voice rising with excitement, "thanks to the wisdom of Justice Brown, the white man *must* turn him away. The law mandates it. The state enforces it. If a wealthy Negro wants a first-class train ticket, the Southern Pacific cannot sell it to him. If he wants a meal, the Galatoire's must bar the door. The white man is now legally obligated to refuse our money."

He looked at Tourgée. "So, Albion... if he cannot buy his ticket from them, he must buy it from... who?"

"Us," Desdunes answered. "We are already incorporating the 'Afro-American Transit Company.' We have the rolling stock ready. We have the routes mapped."

"If he wants life insurance," Martinet went on, "the white companies will now charge him triple or deny him entirely, citing 'actuarial risk.' So he must come to the 'Louisiana Life and Aid Society.'"

"Owned by the Order," Plessy noted.

"If he wants to bury his dead," Martinet listed, ticking off fingers. "If he wants to borrow money for a home. If he wants a haircut. If he wants a hotel room. The white door is closed. The Lumi door is open."

"And the Lumi door," Plessy smiled, "has a toll."

**III. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE INCUBATOR**

Plessy stood and walked to the window, looking out at the street. A streetcar rumbled by—one of the very cars that was now forbidden to him.

"We have created a captive market," Plessy declared, turning back to the room. "Seven million Negroes in the South. They have nowhere else to go. We have fenced them in. We will build a parallel economy. A 'Nation within a Nation.' And because we are the only ones with the capital to build the infrastructure—the banks, the funeral homes, the newspapers, the insurance companies—we will own every aspect of their lives."

"It is a monopoly granted by the Supreme Court," Martinet chuckled. "They think they are segregating us to keep us down. In reality, they are segregating us to let us *concentrate*. They are giving us a walled garden where we can grow our power without their interference. They will not look over the wall because they despise what is inside. And while they look away, we will build a fortress."

Tourgée shook his head. "But the indignity, Homer! The 'Jim Crow' cars. The humiliation of being told you are unfit to sit next to a white man. Think of the psychological toll on the children."

"Indignity is a small price to pay for sovereignty," Plessy shrugged. "Let the common Negro feel the sting. Let him be angry. Let him be humiliated. It will make him militant. It will make him hate the white man. And that hatred... that is energy. That is fuel. We will harness it. We will channel it into our organizations. The NAACP, the Urban League—we will build them all. We will give them a place to pour their rage, and we will use that rage to power our machine."

"The pressure cooker," Desdunes noted. "We enter the era of the Pressure Cooker. The white lid is clamped down tight. The heat rises. And we serve the soup."

**IV. RESOLUTION: THE EXPANSION OF THE TRUST**

"We must move quickly," Plessy said, his demeanor shifting from celebratory to commanding. The playboy demeanor vanished, replaced by the steel of the Grand Architect. "The window is open. The whites are celebrating their 'victory.' They will be lazy. They will think the 'Negro Problem' is solved."

"The Thomy Lafon Trust has authorized the release of funds," Martinet reported. Lafon, the great philanthropist and secret financier of the Order, had left a fortune dedicated to this moment. "We are purchasing real estate in the designated 'Colored' zones of every major Southern city. We are buying the swamp land, the bottoms, the areas they think are worthless."

Plessy nodded. "Atlanta. Memphis. Birmingham. Durham. Richmond. We buy the blocks where the white man won't go. We build our own districts. We build the 'Black Wall Streets.'"

"Black Wall Street," Martinet mused. "It has a nice ring to it."

"We will make them gleam," Plessy promised. "We will build enclaves so rich, so cultured, so self-sufficient that the white man will look over the fence and feel envy. He will see our doctors driving Cadillacs. He will see our women in furs. He will see our banks overflowing with cash. And that envy... that envy will eventually be his undoing. But by then, it will be too late. We will own the debt on his own house."

"And the whites? What do we tell them?" Tourgée asked. "How do we keep them from burning it down?"

"We tell them we are defeated," Plessy grinned. "We tell them we accept our place. We quote Washington. We tell them we are building 'our own little community' to stop bothering them. We play the humble separatist. We say, 'You don't want us? Fine, we will go over here and be quiet.' Segregation is the ultimate camouflage, Albion. It makes us invisible."

**V. AGENDA ITEM THREE: THE AGENT HIMSELF**

Tourgée looked at Plessy with a new understanding, and a new fear. "You planned this. From the beginning. You didn't just want to challenge the law. You wanted to lose."

"I am a Shoemaker, Albion," Plessy said, looking at his fine leather boots. "I know that sometimes you have to cut the leather to make the shoe fit. The 14th Amendment was a beautiful piece of leather, but it didn't fit the foot of the nation. It was too tight. It caused blisters. So we cut it."

He walked over to the lawyer, stopping in front of a mirror. He looked at his own reflection.

"Look at me, Albion. What do you see?"

"I see a white man," Tourgée admitted. "Or... nearly."

"I am seven-eighths white," Plessy said softly. "My great-grandmother was African. The rest? French. Spanish. German. I could pass for white in Paris. I could pass in New York. I could move to London and marry a duchess. I could live a life of ease, free from the burden of the race."

He turned back to the room.

"But I chose to be Black. I chose to be 'Octoroon.' I chose to sit in that car and be arrested. Why? Because the purity you search for is a myth. The Order needed a plaintiff who was undeniably, legally Black, yet visibly White. We needed to show the absurdity of the law, yes, but we also needed to prove that *blood matters more than skin*."

"To trigger the case," Tourgée whispered.

"To trigger the *wall*," Plessy corrected again. "Without the wall, my people—the Creole aristocracy—would drift. They would try to pass. They would assimilate. They would marry into white families and disappear. They would dilute their power, their culture, their blood. They would beg for crumbs at the white man's table. Now? Now the law forces them back. The law says, 'One drop makes you Black.' Good. That means we keep every drop. We keep the genius, the capital, the beauty. We keep it all."

He leaned in close. "I am the gatekeeper, Albion. I shut the gate to the white world, so that we could build our own kingdom behind it."

**VI. THE OATH OF THE GAVEL**

Plessy picked up the morning newspaper, the *New Orleans Picayune*. The headline screamed: *SUPREME COURT UPHOLDS SEGREGATION: SEPARATE BUT EQUAL IS LAW.*

He folded it carefully, creasing the edge with his fingernail.

"This is our charter," he said. "Frame it. Hang it in the boardroom of the Louisiana Life and Aid Society. Hang it in the lobby of the Afro-American Transit Company. The day the white man finalized his divorce from the Negro is the day the Negro married the Lumi."

He raised his glass one last time.

"To Separate!"

"To Separate!" Martinet and Desdunes echoed.

"But Equal!" Plessy added, a wicked glint in his hazel eyes.

"But Equal!" the committee roared with laughter, the sound echoing in the small, velvet-lined room.

**VII. ADJOURNMENT**

Tourgée finished his wine in one gulp. He felt dizzy. He realized he had been playing checkers while these men were playing 4D chess. He gathered his briefcase.

"I will... I will file a petition for a rehearing," Tourgée said weakly. "For the record."

"Do that, Albion," Plessy said, patting him on the back. "Keep the fight alive. Keep the rhetoric flowing. The illusion must be maintained."

Tourgée left the meeting dazed, stepping out into the bright New Orleans sunlight, a man who had won a moral victory in his own mind but lost the strategic war he didn't even know he was fighting.

The Comité des Citoyens remained. Plessy rang a small silver bell. A waiter appeared—a dark-skinned man with intelligent eyes.

"Another bottle, Henri," Plessy ordered. "And bring the ledgers. We have to calculate the projected revenue of the new segregation. I believe the funeral business is going to be very profitable this year."

Henri nodded. "Yes, Grand Architect."

The waiter vanished. The door clicked shut. The wall was up. And inside, the Empire was already counting its gold.
