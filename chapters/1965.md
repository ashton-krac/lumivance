# CHAPTER 23: THE TERMINATION

**DATE:** February 21, 1965  
**LOCATION:** The Projection Booth of the Audubon Ballroom, Washington Heights, New York  
**TIME:** 3:08 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Adam Clayton Powell Jr. (The Harlem Watcher / Council Authority)  

**ATTENDEES (THE CLEANING CREW):**  
1. **Adam Clayton Powell Jr.** - *The Harlem Watcher / Grand Architect Representative*  
2. **Eugene Roberts** - *The Shield (Uninitiated Asset)*  
3. **Anatole Broyard** - *The Passing Critic / Recorder*  
4. **Three Instruments** - *External Cover Assets*  
5. **Malcolm X** - *The Target (On Stage)*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE ROOM THAT KNEW**

"He knows," Eugene Roberts said.

Below the projection booth, the Audubon Ballroom shifted and murmured. Folding chairs scraped. Babies fussed. Men in dark coats watched the doors more than the stage. Women whispered into gloves. The room had the unstable electricity of a place where everyone had been warned and no one had been told the truth.

Malcolm entered from the side.

The applause came late, then hard. He looked thinner than he had in photographs. Tired. The beard sharpened his face. His glasses caught the overhead light and flashed white for a second, hiding his eyes.

Adam Clayton Powell Jr. looked through the slit in the booth wall. He had preached Harlem into a voting bloc, turned the pulpit into a federal platform, and learned that charisma could save people or herd them. Tonight the Order used the same knowledge in reverse.

"Of course he knows," Powell said. "He was trained to read rooms."

Roberts's jaw tightened. "Then call it off."

Broyard turned. His pen paused above the page.

Powell did not look at either of them. "That sentence is why you were never promoted."

Below, Malcolm adjusted the microphone.

"As-salaam alaikum," he said.

The crowd answered.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE ROGUE PROBLEM**

On the narrow shelf beside the projector lay the file: *MALCOLM LITTLE / DETROIT RED / MALCOLM X / EL-HAJJ MALIK EL-SHABAZZ.*

Powell opened it though he knew every page.

"Asset recruited through street observation, 1943 Los Angeles. High pain tolerance. High rhetorical velocity. Exceptional crowd command. Useful rage. Poor submission profile."

Roberts said, "He made men stand up."

"Yes."

"Men the Order ignored."

"The Order used him to reach them."

"You used him until he reached beyond you."

For the first time, Powell turned.

"He has met with African heads of state without clearance. He has proposed taking the American racial question to the United Nations without channel control. He has built lines to Cairo, Accra, Mecca, Algiers. He no longer speaks of civil rights. He speaks of human rights. That changes jurisdiction."

"Maybe jurisdiction needed changing."

Powell closed the file.

"Do not mistake eloquence for governance."

**III. SIDEBAR: THE MAN ON THE STAGE**

Malcolm scanned the ballroom.

He saw the exits blocked by habit, not by furniture. He saw Roberts near the front, pretending to be only another bodyguard. He saw faces he knew from the Nation, faces tightened by instruction. He saw empty seats where allies should have been.

He had been a hustler, prisoner, minister, traitor, pilgrim, husband, father, and symbol. Symbols, he had learned, were rooms other people built around your living body.

He leaned toward the microphone.

"Brothers and sisters..."

His voice was gentler than the room expected.

In the booth, Roberts closed his eyes.

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE COVER**

"The Nation wants him dead," Powell said. "The Bureau wants him contained. The press wants a Black feud. We allow all three to believe they have authored the event."

Broyard wrote: *Layered causality preserves deniability.*

"You make it sound clean," Roberts said.

"Clean is for public records."

"And the children?"

Powell hesitated just long enough for Roberts to see that the question had landed.

Below, Malcolm's daughters sat near the front with their mother.

"Collateral exposure is regrettable," Powell said.

Roberts laughed once. There was no humor in it. "You people can embalm a sin before the body hits the floor."

Powell looked back through the slit. "And yet you remain."

Roberts did not answer.

**V. THE INTERRUPTION**

"Get your hand out of my pocket!"

The shout tore the room diagonally.

Heads turned. Chairs shifted. The old choreography of public disturbance began: look toward noise, lean away from danger, search for authority.

Malcolm raised both hands.

"Now, brothers, break it up. Be cool."

Be cool.

The phrase reached the booth like a private rebuke. Cool had been the Order's armor for decades. Cool meant refusal to react. Cool meant command of the face while the knife entered.

The first instrument stood.

Roberts whispered, "No."

Powell said, "Proceed."

The shotgun appeared from beneath the coat.

**VI. EXECUTION**

The blast hit Malcolm in the chest.

The sound was too large for the room. It flattened the applause still lingering in memory. Malcolm fell backward, arms opening not like surrender but like a man startled by the size of the sky.

Then came the pistols.

People screamed. Chairs overturned. Men lunged and ducked at the same time. Betty Shabazz rose. Roberts moved, but not toward the shooters. Toward the stage. Toward the photograph history would need.

In the booth, Broyard stopped writing.

"Record," Powell said.

"I am."

"Your pen is still."

Broyard looked at the body below. "Then perhaps the record is finally improving."

Powell took the pen and wrote himself.

**VII. SIDEBAR: THE KISS OF JUDAS**

Roberts knelt beside Malcolm and began mouth-to-mouth.

The cameras found him.

That had been part of the design: the loyal protector trying to save the doomed prophet. A picture clean enough for newspapers. Complicated enough for no one to read correctly.

Blood soaked Malcolm's shirt. His glasses were gone. Without them, his face looked younger, almost unguarded.

Roberts breathed into him and tasted iron.

"I'm sorry," he whispered into the mouth of a man who could no longer use words better than anyone alive.

**VIII. RESOLUTION: THE MARTYRDOM PROTOCOL**

"Alive, he was a jurisdictional threat," Powell dictated. "Dead, he becomes a distributable charge."

Broyard resumed writing, but slowly.

"Autobiography publication to proceed. Speeches to be excerpted. Internationalism to be softened for domestic consumption. Image rights to be monitored. Phrase 'by any means necessary' approved for controlled circulation. Anti-Order implications to be diluted through merchandise, classroom excerpts, and anniversary ritual."

Broyard said, "You are not killing him once."

"No," Powell replied. "We are making sure he cannot die uncontrolled."

Below, Betty Shabazz's scream rose above the ballroom.

Powell flinched.

Broyard saw it and wrote that down too.

**IX. THE UNSANCTIONED NOTE**

At 3:47 PM, after the booth had been wiped and the instruments dispersed into the official story, Broyard added a line not approved by Powell:

*The Target was correct about one thing: the Order fears prophets because prophets answer to an authority that cannot be audited.*

He sanded the ink, folded the page, and placed it beneath the authorized minutes.

**X. ADJOURNMENT**

Malcolm X died at Columbia Presbyterian Hospital.

The public explanation began before the blood dried. Feud. Nation. Jealousy. Extremism. Tragedy. Words arranged like furniture to hide the stain.

That night, in Harlem, boys who had never obeyed any organization wept openly in barbershops. In universities, students copied his speeches by hand. In government offices, men who had feared him relaxed too soon.

The Order believed it had converted a rogue asset into a manageable saint.

But martyrdom is a chemical process. It does not always obey the chemist.

*Minutes recorded by Anatole Broyard, sealed under the Passing Critic file.*
