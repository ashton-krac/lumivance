# CHAPTER 27: THE RUMBLE

**DATE:** October 30, 1974  
**LOCATION:** Presidential Compound, Kinshasa, Zaire  
**TIME:** 4:26 AM (Post-Fight)  
**PRESIDING:** Harry Belafonte (The Diaspora Broker)  

**ATTENDEES (THE RESOURCE RECOVERY TEAM):**  
1. **Harry Belafonte** - *Grand Architect / The Diaspora Broker*  
2. **Don King** - *The Promoter (Public Cover)*  
3. **Muhammad Ali** - *The Champion / Uninitiated Conscience*  
4. **Mobutu Sese Seko** - *The Host (Unaffiliated State Power)*  
5. **Frank Yerby** - *The Historical Swashbuckler / Resource Ledger*  
6. **Miriam Makeba** - *The Witness of the Diaspora (Outside Moral Authority)*  
7. **Franco Harris** - *The Immaculate Runner / Extraction Wing*

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: AFTER THE ROAR**

Kinshasa had not slept.

The city still shook with the chant. *Ali bomaye.* Ali kill him. Ali kill him. The words had rolled through the stadium, the streets, the hotel lobbies, the army checkpoints, the kitchens where women washed glasses with water they would not drink. Now, near dawn, the chant had thinned into drums, radios, laughter, and engines coughing behind compound walls.

Muhammad Ali sat with a towel over his head, one eye swelling, ribs bruised deep beneath the skin. He had beaten George Foreman by letting the stronger man spend himself against the ropes. The world called it miracle. Ali knew better. Miracles were what people called strategy when they had not been invited to the planning room.

Don King entered carrying two briefcases and a grin large enough to colonize a smaller country. Behind him came Harry Belafonte, quieter, older in the eyes, a man who had learned that diaspora work required both song and invoice.

"The king is restored," he declared.

Ali lifted the towel. "Which one?"

Mobutu Sese Seko laughed too loudly. He wore leopard skin and exhaustion. Behind him, soldiers stood at attention with guns polished for cameras.

Frank Yerby did not laugh. He unrolled a map across a table still sticky with champagne. The bestselling novelist had made fortunes dressing old empires in romance; now he handled Belgian survey archives with the bitter accuracy of a man who knew adventure stories were often theft with better horses.

"Call to order," Belafonte said.

King's grin sharpened. "Business, then."

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE ROPE-A-DOPE DOCTRINE**

Ali touched the ropes burned into his arms.

"Foreman hit like debt," he said. "Heavy at first. Then slower. Then desperate."

Miriam Makeba stood by the window, listening to the city breathe. "You let the world watch a Black man absorb punishment until power tired itself out."

"That is the West," Yerby said. "Extraction, expansion, exhaustion."

Ali looked at him. "Do not make my bruises into your chart too fast."

Yerby paused. "Correction recorded."

Ali leaned back. "But yes. That is the lesson. Let the empire punch. Cover the ribs. Whisper in its ear. Make it angry. Make it waste oxygen. When it leans in to finish you, show it the floor."

King clapped once. "Poetry with a purse attached."

**III. SIDEBAR: THE FESTIVAL CIRCUIT**

Makeba placed a program from Zaire 74 on the table: James Brown, B.B. King, Bill Withers, Celia Cruz, the Spinners, the Fania All-Stars. Black sound had crossed the Atlantic wearing stage lights and returned to Africa amplified.

"Do not reduce this to rocks," she said.

"Everything reduces to material eventually," Yerby replied.

"Spoken like a man who has never had to sing a scattered people back into one room."

Ali smiled. "Let her cook."

Makeba pointed toward the stadium. "For one night, the Diaspora saw itself not as exile but as circuit. Harlem to Havana to Lagos to Kingston to Kinshasa. The wound did not close, but it conducted."

King nodded impatiently. "And while it conducted, the planes loaded."

Makeba's eyes hardened. "There it is."

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE RESOURCE TRANSFER**

Yerby marked the map: Shinkolobwe, Katanga, rail corridors, private airstrip, Belgian survey archives.

"Two cargo planes," he said. "Ten tons high-grade ore. Cobalt samples. Coltan concessions masked through agricultural equipment contracts. Additional geological records purchased from unpaid Belgian engineers who believe they are selling duplicates."

Mobutu's smile vanished. "Purchased?"

King opened one briefcase. Swiss francs lay in clean bricks.

"A sovereign does not get robbed," King said. "He gets compensated discreetly."

Mobutu looked at the money, then at Ali. "You bring cameras to make me glorious. Then your people leave with my ground."

Ali winced as he stood. Pain made him slower, not smaller.

"Your ground was already marked by men who do not look like either of us," he said. "Belgians. Americans. Companies with names like churches and appetites like armies. The question is not whether the ground leaves. It is who teaches it to come back as power."

Makeba whispered, "And will it come back here?"

No one answered quickly enough.

**V. SIDEBAR: THE MORAL ACCOUNT**

Franco Harris entered, helmet under his arm. He should have been only the gentle running back from Pittsburgh, the man of Black and Italian parentage who turned a deflected ball into the Immaculate Reception. The Order preferred men who could catch what history meant to drop.

"Runway secure. But there are villagers at the perimeter. They know something is moving."

"Pay them," King said.

"They are not asking for money."

The room stilled.

"What then?" Ali asked.

"A well," Franco said. "And a clinic. The old Belgian pump broke three months ago."

Yerby frowned. "That is local administration."

Makeba laughed, cold and bright. "There is always a village under the map, isn't there?"

Ali closed the briefcase and pushed it toward Mobutu.

"A well and a clinic," he said.

King looked pained. "Champ, that's not in the agreement."

"It is now."

Mobutu studied him. "You negotiate while bleeding."

"Best time," Ali said. "People underestimate your arithmetic."

**VI. AGENDA ITEM THREE: PAN-AFRICAN COVER**

The public story would be clean: a fight, a festival, a dictator's spectacle, Ali's resurrection. The hidden story would travel in ore, contracts, and songs.

"The chant matters," Makeba said. "*Ali bomaye* is not just kill him. It is permission. The people gave him symbolic violence and he returned it as victory. Dangerous exchange."

"Can it be replicated?" Yerby asked.

"Not by you."

King laughed. "I can sell anything once."

"That is why you are useful and dangerous," Makeba said.

Ali sat again. His hands shook. No one mentioned it.

"I beat Foreman," he said. "Do not let the minutes say I was only a distraction."

Yerby looked at the map.

"Amendment accepted."

**VII. RESOLUTION: THE KINSHASA ACCORD**

At 5:13 AM, Belafonte's resource recovery desk approved the Kinshasa Accord, with Ali and Makeba forcing the moral amendments into the record:

1. Mineral extraction to proceed under cultural-festival cover.
2. A portion of ore-derived profits reserved for African technical schools selected outside Mobutu's patronage network.
3. Village well and clinic funded immediately.
4. Diaspora festival model approved for future soft-power summits.
5. Rope-a-Dope entered into strategic doctrine: absorb, exhaust, reveal.

King signed first, flamboyant and illegible.

Mobutu signed second.

Ali signed last, slowly, with a hand that had made a fist for half the world.

**VIII. THE OATH OF THE ROPES**

Ali took a strip of ring rope from his robe pocket. He had cut it himself when no one was watching.

"Do you swear," he said, "not to confuse taking with returning?"

Yerby hesitated.

Makeba watched him until he answered.

"I swear."

"Do you swear the Motherland is not merely a warehouse for children who forgot her name?"

"We swear," King said, quieter now.

"Do you swear," Ali said, "to remember that even a champion can be used?"

No one spoke.

Ali tied the rope strip around the map.

"Record the silence," he said.

**IX. ADJOURNMENT**

The planes lifted before sunrise.

In the city, people still chanted. At the stadium, workers swept the aisles. Near the airstrip, villagers watched dark shapes climb into the paling sky and wondered what kind of wealth could leave so early and so guarded.

Ali slept on the flight home with ice on his face.

In the dream, Foreman kept punching. Ali kept leaning. The ropes held.

For now.
