# CHAPTER 25: THE ANTHEM

**DATE:** August 18, 1969  
**LOCATION:** Backstage Trailer, Woodstock Music and Art Fair, Bethel, New York  
**TIME:** 8:41 AM (Monday Morning)  
**PRESIDING:** Jimi Hendrix (The Electric Shaman)  

**ATTENDEES (THE PSYCHEDELIC COUNCIL):**  
1. **Jimi Hendrix** - *The Electric Shaman*  
2. **Billy Cox** - *The Anchor (Band Ally / Uninitiated)*  
3. **Mitch Mitchell** - *The Timekeeper (Band Ally / Uninitiated)*  
4. **Etta James** - *The Blues Chemist / Pain Register*  
5. **August Wilson** - *The Frequency Scribe / Street Ear*  
6. **Four Hundred Thousand Children of the Empire** - *Unwitting Choir*

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: MUD CHURCH**

"Half of them left," Billy Cox said.

Jimi Hendrix looked through the trailer window at the field. Monday morning had thinned the nation. What remained was mud, scaffolding, abandoned blankets, dazed white children, Black musicians with red eyes, vendors counting damp bills, and the sour-sweet smell of a civilization that had mistaken exhaustion for freedom.

"Enough stayed," Jimi said.

He sat with the white Stratocaster across his knees, tuning by ear because the machines lied when the air was this wet. His jacket hung open. His hands were beautiful and restless. Every few seconds he played a harmonic so soft it seemed less like sound than a question.

Etta James placed the festival chemist's metal case on the table. Her platinum hair was pinned beneath a scarf, her eyes sharp with the old gospel-blues suspicion of any man who mistook altered feeling for truth.

"Distribution was successful," she read from the report. "Owsley batches through the west field. Lower concentration near the stage. Higher suggestibility among the front clusters. Sleep deprivation has done the rest."

Mitch Mitchell rubbed his face. "You make it sound like crop rotation."

"It is, if you are ugly enough to say it plain," Etta said.

Jimi looked at her then. "Careful."

Etta blinked.

"Those are people out there," Jimi said. "Lost, spoiled, beautiful, stupid, scared people. But people."

Etta closed the case.

"Correction recorded."

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE STAR-SPANGLED DECONSTRUCTION**

August Wilson spread a frequency sheet across the amp case. He was not famous yet, not to the country, not even to most of Pittsburgh outside the places where men argued themselves into poetry, but he could hear a crowd the way a playwright hears a block before he knows its plot.

"If you begin with 'Voodoo Child,' we can hold the field. If you close with 'Hey Joe,' they leave satisfied. If you play the anthem..."

"They don't leave satisfied," Jimi said.

"No."

"Good."

Billy watched him. "You sure?"

Jimi smiled faintly. "America plays that song like a curtain. Everybody stands up and stops seeing what is behind it. I want to pull it down and let the machinery show."

He bent a note until it cried like metal under heat.

"Bombs," he said. "Sirens. Mothers. Helicopters. The high whine a boy hears before the jungle opens. All of it is already inside the melody. They just play it too clean."

Mitch tapped a stick against his thigh. "And the kids?"

"They came here to escape their parents' country. I am going to give it back to them with blood on it."

**III. SIDEBAR: THE ELECTRIC CHURCH**

The Order had tested voice through Marian, rhythm through Ellington, crowd silence through the March, and bodily frequency through Prince's still-unborn dreams in Minneapolis. But the electric guitar was a different instrument. It did not persuade. It invaded.

August adjusted the notation.

"Feedback at the upper edge of pain. Not enough to empty the field. Enough to make them feel the flag in the teeth."

"The teeth remember," Jimi said.

Etta nodded toward the chemist's report. "The acid loosens symbolic attachment. A sacred object can be rewritten if the subject encounters it during ego dissolution."

"There that report goes again," Billy said.

Jimi laughed. "It means they're high enough to hear the truth."

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: PACIFICATION OR LIBERATION**

A woman from the festival staff entered with coffee. She was barefoot, white, nineteen, and sunburned. She froze when she saw the documents.

Etta reached for them.

"Let her see," Jimi said.

She looked from face to face. "Is this... political?"

Mitch laughed. "It's the national anthem."

"Then yeah," she said. "I guess."

Jimi held out the guitar pick. "What do you want to hear?"

The girl thought he was flirting and then realized he was not.

"Something that makes it stop," she said.

"What?"

She gestured helplessly at the field, the news, the war, the draft board waiting at home, the boys she knew who had gone to Vietnam and come back mean or not at all.

"The whole machine," she said.

Etta smiled without warmth. "Excellent subject response."

Jimi's face hardened. "Leave."

Etta hesitated.

"I said leave."

After she stepped out, Jimi looked at the girl.

"I can't make it stop," he said. "But I can make it impossible to pretend it sounds pretty."

She nodded. That was enough.

**V. RESOLUTION: THE COUNTER-CULTURE CAPTURE**

When the girl left, Etta returned, chastened and irritated.

"The objective remains: sever elite youth from inherited patriotic reflex. Redirect them toward hedonism, antiwar sentiment, racial openness, and distrust of state authority."

"You mean make them useless to their fathers," Billy said.

"Useful to themselves," Jimi said.

"Useful to us," Etta corrected.

Jimi stood, the guitar cable dragging behind him like a black rope.

"No," he said. "The Order keeps thinking music is a leash. It is not. It is a door. You can open it. You cannot decide who walks through or what they become outside."

Mitch looked relieved. "That sounds harder to put in the minutes."

"Good," Jimi said.

**VI. THE PERFORMANCE**

The stage was wet.

The crowd looked less like an army than survivors of a flood. A few flags hung limp. Smoke rose from breakfast fires. Someone was asleep on a sheet of plywood. Someone else was crying and laughing into the same hand.

Jimi stepped to the microphone.

He did not explain.

The first notes of "The Star-Spangled Banner" came out recognizable enough to gather the field. Heads lifted. Bodies turned. An old reflex tried to stand inside them.

Then he wounded it.

The melody bent. The bombs entered. The rockets did not glare; they screamed. The whammy bar pulled the flag through napalm, radio static, riot sirens, mothers praying, boys burning draft cards, boys burning villages, feedback opening like a white phosphorus flower.

For four minutes, America heard itself without orchestration.

Some cheered because they thought it was rebellion.

Some stood stunned because they knew it was mourning.

Some hated him for touching the song.

Some loved him for finally touching it hard enough.

Backstage, Etta watched the field and whispered, "Suggestibility confirmed."

Billy Cox said, "Shut up."

**VII. THE OATH OF FEEDBACK**

Afterward, Jimi sat on the stage steps, sweat cooling under his jacket.

"Did it work?" August asked.

Jimi looked out over the mud.

"Depends what you think work is."

"The patriotic association is damaged."

"So is the boy playing it."

No one answered.

Jimi wrapped the cable around his arm.

"Record this," he said.

August lifted the field recorder.

"The anthem belongs to whoever can survive playing the truth inside it."

**VIII. ADJOURNMENT**

By afternoon, the field was emptying. By the end of the week, critics would call the performance desecration, genius, noise, prophecy. The Order would file it as a successful severance operation. The counter-culture would carry it like a burn scar.

Jimi slept badly that night.

In his dream, the anthem kept trying to become clean again.

He kept bending it until it confessed.
