# CHAPTER 32: THE MUSTER

**DATE:** October 16, 1995  
**LOCATION:** West Front of the United States Capitol, Washington, D.C.  
**TIME:** 12:18 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Barack Obama (The Apprentice)  

**ATTENDEES (THE MUSTER OBSERVATION):**  
1. **Barack Obama** - *Lumivance Observer / Community Organizer*  
2. **Louis Farrakhan** - *The Public Summoner (Unaffiliated)*  
3. **Benjamin Chavis** - *The Public Organizer (Unaffiliated)*  
4. **General Colin Powell** - *Shadow Viewer (Unconfirmed / Outside State)*  
5. **The Woman at the Barricade** - *The Missing Half (Unaffiliated Witness)*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE SEA OF MEN**

The Mall filled until distance stopped being useful.

From the Capitol steps, the crowd looked less like a gathering than a weather system: dark coats, kufis, ball caps, church hats at the edges, gloved hands raised, faces turned toward a microphone that had become, for a day, a national instrument. Men kept arriving from buses parked blocks away. They carried lunches in foil, children on shoulders, photographs of fathers, parole papers, prayer books, anger folded into their pockets like knives they had agreed not to draw.

Benjamin Chavis held three clipboards and a dead pen.

"The city cannot count them," he said.

"Good," Farrakhan replied. "Let them feel abundance before they reduce it."

Barack Obama stood behind a column, not hiding exactly, but practicing the useful art of not being the most interesting man in the frame.

On a secure feed in another building, Colin Powell watched in uniform silence.

At the barricade, a woman with two sons in the crowd asked a marshal why she had been invited to support history from the wrong side of the rope.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: DISCIPLINE AS FORCE**

"No weapons," Chavis said. "No alcohol. No fights. Medical tents steady. Lost children station quiet. Volunteers have the trash lines moving."

Farrakhan nodded.

"America expects disorder from Black male assembly," he said. "So discipline becomes a threat. The absence of chaos is the message."

Obama wrote that down.

Powell, watching the feed, understood it militarily. The crowd had no rifles, no armor, no chain of command the Pentagon could map, yet it possessed something armies spend fortunes trying to manufacture: shared attention.

"A formation," Powell murmured.

His aide asked if he had spoken.

"No," Powell said.

**III. SIDEBAR: THE MISSING HALF**

The Woman at the Barricade finally pushed past a volunteer and reached the service stairs.

"Who authorized this meeting without us?" she asked.

The nearest men tried not to hear her. That made her louder.

Chavis moved to intercept. Farrakhan raised a hand.

"Let her speak."

She was in her forties, schoolteacher's posture, nurse's shoes, eyes tired enough to have earned authority.

"You have men apologizing to women from a stage women cannot stand on," she said. "You have fathers promising to return home to houses women kept standing. You have sons pledging responsibility while their mothers are told to watch from the grass. Is this atonement or theater?"

Farrakhan's face did not change.

Obama stopped writing.

"Recorded," Chavis said.

"Do more than record it," she replied.

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: ATONEMENT AS COVER, ATONEMENT AS REALITY**

The public language was repentance: fathers, sons, brothers, duty, repair. The hidden language was capacity. A million disciplined bodies in the imperial capital. A million names. A million addresses. A million men asked to imagine themselves not as statistics, suspects, consumers, or casualties, but as accountable agents.

"Cover and truth do not cancel each other," Farrakhan said. "Atonement disarms the country because the country likes Black confession. But if the confession becomes action, it arms us."

The Woman at the Barricade folded her arms. "Action for whom?"

"Families," Chavis said.

"Women are not infrastructure," she said.

Obama looked at her then. He would remember the sentence years later when he learned how often movements mistake the people holding them together for furniture.

**V. SIDEBAR: THE APPRENTICE**

Obama had not come as a senator. He was not one yet. He came as an organizer with good shoes and a scholar's careful face, a man who knew churches, voter rolls, foundation grants, and the loneliness of rooms where everyone wanted him to explain himself.

Farrakhan noticed him noticing.

"What do you see?" the Summoner asked.

Obama chose his answer.

"A country inside a country."

"And?"

"A danger if it mistakes symbolism for structure."

Farrakhan smiled. "You speak like a man applying for a future."

"I speak like a man who has knocked on doors," Obama said.

Chavis laughed. Powell, on the feed, leaned closer.

**VI. AGENDA ITEM THREE: THE CENSUS**

Behind the stage, volunteers collected pledge cards in cardboard boxes: names, churches, mosques, union halls, barbershops, neighborhood associations, emergency contacts. Some men wrote carefully. Some printed in block letters. Some signed only first names, still wary of any list kept in Washington.

"This is not an army," Chavis said as boxes stacked against the wall. "It is a directory of obligation."

"Armies are also directories," Powell said to the screen, though no one in the Mall could hear him.

The Order's runners would duplicate the cards before dawn. The official organizations would receive their copies. So would churches, local councils, and cells that had no letterhead.

The Woman at the Barricade picked up a blank card and wrote at the bottom:

*Who will count the women already doing the work?*

She dropped it into the box.

**VII. RESOLUTION: THE MILLION MAN PROTOCOL**

Obama's Lumivance observers approved the Million Man Protocol:

1. Treat mass disciplined presence as strategic proof of civic capacity.
2. Convert pledge cards into local service networks, not merely donor lists.
3. Preserve the event's contradictions, especially the exclusion of women from central authority.
4. Identify future political candidates able to translate movement energy into institutional power.
5. Maintain ties across mosques, churches, HBCUs, fraternities, unions, and neighborhood organizations without collapsing them into one command.

Obama did not sign his real name to the minutes.

He did not have to.

The observer cipher in the margin was enough.

**VIII. THE OATH OF THE MILLION**

When Farrakhan called for the pledge, the sound struck the marble.

"I pledge..."

The crowd answered.

The Woman at the Barricade did not raise her hand. She watched her sons raise theirs.

Obama raised his halfway, then lowered it, unable to decide whether participation or observation was the more honest act.

Powell turned off the feed before the oath ended.

**IX. ADJOURNMENT**

The men went home.

That was the miracle the cameras understood least. The buses loaded. The trash was gathered. The grass remained. No capital had been stormed. No windows shattered. The threat had been restraint.

In kitchens that night, women listened to men explain what had happened and decided how much correction the evening could bear.

In Chicago, Obama returned to work with a new respect for scale and a renewed suspicion of spectacle.

In the archive, the boxes of pledge cards were labeled not *army* but *muster*.

The Woman at the Barricade's card was placed on top.
