# CHAPTER 29: THE VIOLET FREQUENCY

**DATE:** July 26, 1984  
**LOCATION:** Basement Studio, First Avenue, Minneapolis, Minnesota  
**TIME:** 4:03 AM  
**PRESIDING:** Prince Rogers Nelson (The Purple One)  

**ATTENDEES (THE REVOLUTION):**  
1. **Prince** - *The Purple One*  
2. **Wendy Melvoin** - *The Tuner (Band Ally / Uninitiated)*  
3. **Lisa Coleman** - *The Counter-Melody (Band Ally / Uninitiated)*  
4. **Morris Day** - *The Mirror (Public Rival / Uninitiated)*  
5. **Lenny Kravitz** - *The Sonic Heir / Frequency Operations*  
6. **The Kid on Screen** - *The Vessel (Fictional Projection)*

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: PURPLE LIGHT**

"The snare is too dry," Prince said.

No one had slept. The studio smelled of sweat, ozone, hairspray, cigarette ash, and tape warmed by too many passes over the heads. Purple gels covered the lamps, tinting skin, chrome, and white walls until everyone in the room looked like they had been dipped in dusk.

Lenny Kravitz checked the board. He was twenty, not yet the retro-future rock prophet he would become, but Roxie Roker's son already carried television, Blackness, Jewishness, and amplifier dust as if genre had failed to inform him of its borders. "We can add plate reverb."

Prince turned slowly.

"I did not say make it wet like a bathroom," he said. "I said make it wet like a secret."

Morris Day laughed from the couch. "That's why nobody likes working with you."

"Everybody likes working with me," Prince said. "They just discover new kinds of fear."

Wendy and Lisa exchanged the glance of people who knew both statements were true.

On the small monitor, the final performance sequence from *Purple Rain* waited in grainy pause: The Kid, guitar in hand, face lit like confession.

Prince pointed at the screen. "Again."

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE ANDROGYNY PROTOCOL**

Lenny opened the frequency log.

"Crossover response is unprecedented. Rock radio, R&B radio, pop retail, church girls, biker boys, white suburbs, Black clubs. They cannot place you."

"Good," Prince said.

"The ambiguity is functioning as designed."

Prince's eyes narrowed. "Careful."

Lenny looked up.

"Ambiguity is not a costume I put on for market penetration," Prince said. "It is the place I live because your categories are cheap apartments."

Lisa smiled into her coffee.

Wendy added, "They keep asking whether the Revolution is Black or white."

"Tell them yes," Prince said.

Morris adjusted his cuffs. "And male or female?"

"Tell them yes twice."

Lenny wrote: *Binary refusal increases audience capture.*

Prince leaned over and crossed it out.

He wrote: *Freedom sounds like confusion to the trained ear.*

**III. SIDEBAR: THE MINNEAPOLIS SOUND**

They played the track again.

Drum machine. Synths. Guitar like weather. Gospel ache over industrial precision. No single tradition could claim the architecture. That was the point.

"No bass line here," Lenny said. "The absence creates tension."

"The body supplies the bass," Prince said. "Make them participate."

Wendy listened with her head tilted. "The chord wants to resolve and doesn't."

"So do I," Prince said.

The room went quiet, but he had already moved on.

"Minneapolis is perfect," he continued. "Too cold for lazy funk. Too white for comfort. Too Black to disappear. We build the sound in the middle of nowhere so the coasts have to come inland to kneel."

Morris lifted a glass. "To nowhere."

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE VIOLET FREQUENCY**

Lenny lowered his voice.

"We embedded the carrier in the guitar solo. The overtone cluster around the sustained bend produces measurable skin response. Subjects report grief, sexual release, religious memory, and forgiveness."

"Forgiveness is dangerous," Lisa said.

Prince nodded. "Only if it arrives before ownership."

"Ownership of what?" Morris asked.

"The self. The masters. The name. The body. The publishing. The room."

Lenny looked nervous. "Warner believes they own the masters."

Prince smiled.

No one in the room liked that smile except Prince.

**V. SIDEBAR: THE SLAVE CONTRACT**

"They will offer more," Lenny said. "Money, films, distribution, lifetime structure."

"Structure is another word for cage with good lighting," Wendy said.

Prince sat at the piano and played three notes that sounded like a door deciding whether to open.

"I will sign what I need to sign," he said. "Then I will make the cage famous. I will write the word on my face if I have to. I will become unpronounceable. I will show every artist after me that a name can be a plantation."

Morris stopped smiling.

"That sounds lonely."

Prince did not look up. "So does genius when the room is honest."

**VI. AGENDA ITEM THREE: THE CONFESSIONAL TRAP**

On the monitor, The Kid's father struck, The Kid performed, Apollonia watched, the crowd forgave.

Lisa folded her arms. "The women still absorb too much of his damage."

Prince turned from the piano. "The film knows that."

"Does it?"

He looked at the screen for a long time.

"Maybe not enough."

Wendy said, "Then the music has to know."

Prince nodded once. "The song apologizes where the script cannot."

Lenny wrote that down.

Prince did not cross it out.

**VII. RESOLUTION: PAISLEY PARK PRE-CONSTRUCTION**

At 5:15 AM, Prince and Lenny approved the next phase, while the band allies heard only the parts that belonged to the music:

1. Deploy *Purple Rain* as crossover proof of racial-frequency collapse.
2. Begin land acquisition outside Minneapolis for a self-contained production sanctuary.
3. Preserve alternate mixes containing pure Violet carriers in sealed archive.
4. Prepare future ownership revolt against label control.
5. Monitor fan response for signs of unsanctioned devotional dependency.

"A sanctuary?" Morris asked.

"Paisley Park," Prince said.

"Sounds like a daycare."

"Good," Prince said. "Let them underestimate the nursery."

**VIII. THE OATH OF THE RAIN**

They stood around the console as the final solo played.

Prince placed one hand on the master tape.

"Do you swear," he said, "to make no border between flesh and spirit, Black and white, man and woman, machine and church?"

"We swear," Wendy and Lisa said.

Morris sighed. "We swear."

"Do you swear that if they own the record, they do not own the sound?"

"We swear."

"Do you swear," Lisa added, "to remember that confession without change is performance?"

Prince looked at her, then bowed.

"I swear."

**IX. ADJOURNMENT**

By sunrise, the mix still was not finished. It never would be, not in Prince's mind. Finished was a word for people without vaults.

When the album reached the world, lighters rose in arenas like small obedient stars. White rock stations played a Black man in lace until their formats broke. Church girls cried. Boys bought guitars. Executives counted money and mistook revenue for ownership.

In the basement at First Avenue, a strip of purple tape remained stuck to the console.

On it, in Prince's handwriting:

*The frequency is not theirs just because they hear it.*

*Minutes recorded by Wendy Melvoin, The Tuner.*
