# CHAPTER 28: THE ASSASSINATION

**DATE:** September 21, 1980  
**LOCATION:** Essex House Hotel, New York City  
**TIME:** 10:11 AM (Before the Central Park Run)  
**PRESIDING:** Harry Belafonte (The Zion Broker)  

**ATTENDEES (THE ZION COUNCIL):**  
1. **Harry Belafonte** - *Grand Architect / The Zion Broker*  
2. **Bob Marley** - *The Prophet (Outside Conduit / Jamaican Witness)*  
3. **Rita Marley** - *The Protector (Family Authority / Unaffiliated)*  
4. **Peter Tosh** - *The Skeptic (Outside Conscience)*  
5. **Anatole Broyard** - *The Passing Critic / Unverified Knife*  
6. **Dr. Jane Cooke Wright** - *Medical Liaison / Oncology Desk*  
7. **Shari Belafonte** - *Keeper of the Masters*

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE GIFT**

"Jah live," Bob Marley said, and coughed into a white handkerchief.

For a moment everyone looked at the cloth.

No blood. Not today.

The hotel room faced Central Park, but the curtains were half-drawn against the morning glare. Guitars leaned against the wall. A soccer ball sat under a chair. On the table were tea, rolling papers, a bowl of fruit no one had touched, and a pair of boots wrapped in tissue paper.

Harry Belafonte stood near the curtains, far enough from the box to refuse ownership of it. He had learned to move money, microphones, and men across borders, but Marley made even Belafonte's internationalism feel too earthly.

Anatole Broyard smiled with too many teeth. In New York, he made sentences sharp enough to pass as innocence; in Order rooms, his talent for crossing lines made him useful and distrusted in equal measure.

"A gift," he said. "For the concert. Italian leather. Very fine."

Peter Tosh did not touch the box. "Babylon brings shoes now?"

"A token of respect," Broyard said.

Rita watched Bob watching the boots.

Bob's face was calm, but his fingers had stopped moving. That was how she knew.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE EXODUS THREAT**

Shari Belafonte turned on the recorder hidden inside an ashtray.

Broyard opened his notebook. "They say you intend to speak tonight about repatriation."

Bob smiled. "They say many things."

"A mass movement. Jamaica. Ethiopia. Ghana. Zimbabwe. Black people returning. Is that practical?"

"Practical?" Peter snapped. "Slavery was practical. Colonization was practical. Practical is the word thieves use after they build the fence."

Bob lifted a hand. Peter quieted, barely.

"Exodus is not only geography," Bob said. "It is mind leaving captivity."

Broyard wrote nothing. "But if millions believed you literally?"

"Then the labor markets tremble," Bob said.

Broyard looked up.

Bob laughed softly. "You think Rasta do not understand economics? Babylon depends on the people never hearing the same drum at the same time. Reggae makes the drum portable."

**III. SIDEBAR: THE BOOT STORY**

The official story had many versions. A soccer injury. A toe wound. A melanoma missed or mistrusted. A boot with a wire. A gift with a needle. A disease already waiting inside the body. The Order never fully resolved the origin, and that was unusual. Lumivance preferred clean causality. Bob Marley lived in a theology that made clean causality look childish.

Rita moved the boots away from him.

"No," Bob said.

"Robert."

"Bring them."

Peter stood. "You mad?"

Bob looked at him. "Maybe."

Dr. Jane Cooke Wright stepped forward. "Your immune system is compromised. Any puncture, any contaminant-"

"Man already punctured," Bob said, touching his chest. "Question is who chooses the song coming out."

Rita's eyes filled, but she did not let tears fall in front of strangers.

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE LEGEND PROBLEM**

When Broyard went to the bathroom, the room changed.

Peter moved fast, checking the boots. Dr. Wright took a sample from the inner seam. Shari photographed the stitching.

"If this is what we think," Peter said, "we kill him now."

Rita looked toward the bathroom door.

Bob shook his head.

"No."

"No?" Peter's voice rose. "You want martyrdom so bad?"

"I want the music to outrun the men."

"You living helps the music."

"Sometimes," Bob said.

Rita crossed the room and struck him across the face.

The sound was small and shocking.

"Do not make your death into a strategy while I am standing here," she said.

Bob accepted the blow. His cheek reddened.

"You right," he said.

No one moved.

"Then fight," she said.

"I am."

"Not like a prophet. Like a husband."

For the first time that morning, he looked afraid.

**V. SIDEBAR: THE CENTRAL PARK TEST**

Dr. Wright read the preliminary strip. "Trace isotope. Not enough to prove. Enough to worry."

"Could be industrial," Shari said.

"Could be message," Peter replied.

Bob took the boots and held them in his lap.

"Babylon always gives you the thing you already want," he said. "Shoes for the road. Microphone for the mouth. Crown for the head. Contract for the song. Then asks why you complain about the chain."

He put the boots down.

"I will run barefoot."

Rita exhaled. It was almost a laugh.

**VI. AGENDA ITEM THREE: REDEMPTION SONG**

Shari opened a separate case. Inside were master tapes, duplicates, handwritten lyrics.

"If they move against you," she said, "the archive leaves tonight."

"Where?"

"Three copies. London, Kingston, Lagos."

Bob nodded. "And 'Redemption Song'?"

"Protected."

"No band on that one," Bob said. "Just voice and wood. They cannot assassinate a man alone with a guitar. He already sound like ghost."

Peter said, "You are not dead yet."

"No," Bob answered. "That is why we record instructions."

**VII. RESOLUTION: THE ZION DISPERSAL**

Bob and the Zion archive team approved, with Rita's protection clause binding:

1. Marley master tapes duplicated and dispersed.
2. Repatriation speech softened publicly, preserved in full for movement channels.
3. Medical security increased without public panic.
4. The boots retained as evidence, not symbol.
5. Legend Protocol delayed: no martyrdom language while subject lives.

Rita insisted on the fifth clause.

Bob signed it.

**VIII. THE OATH OF ONE HEART**

Bob placed his palm on the guitar.

"Do you swear," he said, "not to turn unity into a brand with no road under it?"

"We swear," Shari said.

"Do you swear the song belongs to the sufferers before the collectors?"

"We swear," Peter said.

"Do you swear," Rita said, taking over, "that if he tries to float away into prophecy, you will pull him back by the ankle?"

Bob laughed until he coughed.

"We swear," everyone said.

**IX. ADJOURNMENT**

Bob did not wear the boots.

He ran later than planned, slower than he wanted, with Rita watching from the path and Peter pretending not to. He collapsed before the concert season was done. The disease advanced. The official explanations multiplied. So did the songs.

The Order filed the event as probable assassination pressure, failed immediate termination, successful dispersal of Zion archive.

Rita filed it differently:

*He chose life that morning. History should not steal that from him.*
