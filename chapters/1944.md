# CHAPTER 17: A TROJAN HORSE

**DATE:** September 24, 1944  
**LOCATION:** The Cockpit of a P-51 Mustang ("Red Tail"), 30,000 feet over Berlin  
**TIME:** 1300 Hours  
**PRESIDING:** Grand Architect Benjamin O. Davis Jr. (Commander)  

**ATTENDEES (THE RED TAIL SQUADRON - IN FLIGHT):**  
1. **Benjamin O. Davis Jr.** - *Red Leader*  
2. **Roscoe Brown** - *Red Two*  
3. **Lee Archer** - *Red Three*  
4. **The Supply Drone** (Experimental Cargo Plane) - *The Package*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER**

"Red Leader to Squadron," the voice of Benjamin O. Davis Jr. crackled through the static, crisp and devoid of the fatigue that gnawed at the edges of his consciousness. "Switch to Frequency Omega. Lumivance Secure Channel."

The command cut through the droning roar of the Rolls-Royce Merlin engine that vibrated through every inch of the P-51 Mustang. At thirty thousand feet, the air was thin and mercilessly cold, a physical weight that pressed against the Plexiglas canopy with an intimacy that felt almost violated. Davis adjusted the oxygen mask tight against his face, breathing in the sterile, rubber-tasting air that kept his brain firing in an environment that wanted him dead. His gloved hands, thick with sheepskin lining, rested lightly on the stick, sensing the microscopic tremors of the airframe—the shimmer of the aluminum skin against the slipstream. He checked his gauges: oil pressure nominal at 80 psi, manifold pressure steady, fuel tanks feeding correctly from the fuselage. He was a machine inside a machine, suspended in the blue void above a burning continent.

Below them, the world was ending. Berlin was a smoldering grid of geography, obscured by the black and gray pillars of smoke rising from the railyards and heavy industry sectors. The B-17 Flying Fortresses, the "Aluminum Overcast," stretched out in a terrifyingly disciplined formation ahead of him—hundreds of silver crosses drifting slowly through the flak bursts that bloomed like ugly black flowers in the sky. The concussion of the larger shells could be felt even here, a dull thump against the chest cavity, a reminder that the air itself was weaponized.

"Red Two, confirmed," came the reply from Roscoe Brown, his tone level, though Davis could hear the tight, shallow breaths in the background, the tell of a man fighting the thin air and the psychological weight of the mesmerizing destruction below.
"Red Three, confirmed," Lee Archer added, his voice sharpened by the adrenaline of the altitude, eager, perhaps too eager, for the violence that was their trade.

The channel switched with a heavy auditory click. The chaotic chatter of the white bomber crews—panic, coordinates, prayers, curses—vanished instantly. The silence that replaced it was not empty; it was heavy with intent. They were now in the private airspace of the Order. The chaotic, muddy war below belonged to the Allies. The war up here, in the cold, clear ether, belonged to Lumivance.

Davis looked to his left and right. The other Mustangs, their tails painted a defiant, blood-red, held their positions with geometric perfection. They did not bob or weave; they hung in the sky as if mounted on rails, a testament to thousands of hours of drill that no white squadron had ever been forced to endure.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE DUAL MISSION**

"Primary objective: Protect the Bombers," Davis recited, his eyes scanning the horizon for the glint of enemy interceptors. "We have never lost a bomber. It is a statistic that defies the probability of this war, but we do not deal in probability. We deal in certainty."

He watched the B-17s plodding along, lumbering beasts that relied on mass for survival. Inside those pressurized tins were ten men each—boys from Iowa who had never seen an ocean, mechanics from Detroit, farmhands from Alabama who would not share a water fountain with him back in Tuskegee. Up here, he was their god. It was a dynamic he savored, a bitter nectar that fueled him more than the high-octane gasoline in his tanks.

"The white pilots call us 'Red Tail Angels,'" Davis continued, a dry rhythm to his speech. "They trust us now. They depend on us. It is a fragile trust, born of desperation, but we shall exploit it. Every man we return to his mother is a deposit in the ledger of moral debt. They will owe us their lives, and one day, we will come to collect."

"Received, Red Leader," Brown said. "Though I admit, it tests the spirit to guard men who would lynch me if we were on the ground. I saw the way the crew of the 'Memphis Belle' looked at us on the tarmac. Not with gratitude. With confusion. They cannot reconcile the savior with the slave."

"That is the discipline," Davis corrected him sharply, his voice cutting through the intercom. "Let them have the dirt. We have the sky. Gravity does not discriminate, and neither do we. Maintain proximity. Keep the formation tight. Your personal feelings are baggage; jettison them."

"Secondary objective: The Drop," Davis transitioned, his voice dropping an octave, shifting from the Commander of the 332nd to the Grand Architect of the Order. "Target coordinates: Sector 7G. The Black Forest."

He banked the plane slightly, five degrees to the port side, allowing him to see the gap between the third and fourth bomber wings. Tucked dangerously close to the belly of a B-24 Liberator was a smaller, unmarked craft—the Supply Drone. It was a modified transport, stripped of armament to carry maximum payload, painted a matte black that seemed to absorb the high-altitude light. It flew in the 'wash'—the turbulent air behind the bomber's propellers—making it nearly invisible to ground radar, masked by the larger signature of the fortress.

"We have visual on the Package," Archer reported. "The unmarked cargo plane is in position. It is shadowing the bomber wing effectively. The bomber crews have not noticed the parasite craft. The turbulence is severe, however. The drone pilot is earning his keep."

"Good," Davis said. "The chaos of the flak provides necessary cover. We separate in thirty seconds. Brown, Archer—you are on me. We execute the weave maneuver to screen the departure. We must act as a visual shield between the drone and the bomber gunners. If a tail gunner sees a black plane detached from the formation, he will panic. He will shoot."

**III. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE SUPPLY DROP**

As the main bomber force began its slow turn away from the target zone, dropping their ordnance into the inferno below, the Red Tails executed a maneuver of subtle divergence. They did not break away violently; they drifted, appearing to chase a phantom bogey, putting distance between themselves and the white pilots.

The physical sensation of the turn was crushing. Davis felt the G-suit squeeze his legs, forcing blood back into his upper body. He fought the stick, the Mustang wanting to climb, but he forced it down, matching the descent of the Supply Drone. The engine pitch changed, a shallow growl as they descended into thicker air.

They surrounded the smaller, darker plane as it dipped its nose, descending rapidly toward the dense, dark green canopy of the Black Forest, far to the south of the burning capital. It was not dropping bombs. The bay doors opened, and canisters, streamlined and heavy, tumbled out. They did not fall randomly; they were timed releases, calculating wind drift and air density.

"Contents?" Roscoe Brown asked, watching the packages fall away, black specks against the white clouds.

"Gold," Davis listed, ticking the items off in his mind, visualizing the heavy bars stamped with the Reichsadler. "Swiss Francs, minted in 1930—untraceable currency. Microfilm containing the complete telemetry data of the V2 rocket program. And the viral cultures—the serum required for the French Resistance sleeper cells in Lyon."

"The Nazis are collapsing," Brown noted. "The carcass is rotting before the beast is even dead."

"We are looting the burning house before the fire department arrives," Davis said. "The Allies fight for territory. The Russians fight for ideology. The Order extracts the intellectual capital of the Reich. Von Braun’s data. The jet engine schematics. The heavy water research. We are stealing the future, gentlemen. While the world celebrates the end of the war, we are financing the next century. This gold will build the endowments of Morehouse and Spelman. This data will build the rockets that will carry the first American to space—rockets that will bear the hidden signature of the Order. It is the endowment of our sovereignty."

"Bombers did not see a thing," Archer laughed, the sound distinct over the radio. "They are too busy watching the flak and praying for home. The Package is away. Trajectory is clean. Reception team is signaled."

Davis watched the Supply Drone level out and turn toward the neutral Swiss border. A perfect insertion. The wealth of a dying empire, transferred into the coffers of the Third Race. It was a theft of biblical proportions, conducted in plain sight. It was the only reparations they would ever receive, so they took them by force.

**IV. RESOLUTION: THE PRICE OF EXCELLENCE**

"Form up," Davis ordered. "We return to the escort. The bombers will be vulnerable on the egress."

The Mustangs throttled up, the Merlin engines screaming as they climbed back to altitude. The climb was brutal. The engine temperature gauges spiked, the needles dancing in the red. Davis feathered the throttle, coaxing the machine, treating it not as a tool but as an extension of his own vascular system. He could feel the heat radiating through the firewall.

"We fly better than them," Roscoe Brown muttered, the resentment seeping back into his voice, fueled by the exhaustion of the climb. "We shoot straighter. We navigate by stars they cannot even name. We save their lives every day. And when we land at Ramitelli, I simply wish to have a drink at the Officer’s Club without the MP asking for my papers. I wish to sit in a chair that does not have to be wiped down after I leave."

"Do not covet their club, Roscoe," Davis said, his voice hard as iron. "It is a room of wood and smoke. It is small. Look around you." He rolled his plane on its axis, the world spinning—the blue sky, the white clouds, the burning earth. "We have the Horizon. Excellence is our revenge. It is the only weapon they cannot confiscate. Every bomber we bring home safely is a proof of our superiority. They will try to deny it. They will try to bury the history. But they cannot bury the fact that when the sky tried to kill them, it was a Black hand that pulled them back. That is power, Roscoe. Real power. Not the petty power of a club membership. We are building a kingdom in the air because they will not let us build one on the ground."

"They will try to disband us," Archer warned. "The War Department already writes the memos. They fear a Negro with a plane. They fear what we have become up here. They fear that we will not land."

"They cannot disband what they need," Davis said. "We have proven that the air has no race. But the machine gun does. And ours are loaded."

**V. THE OATH OF THE ALTITUDE**

"Bandits at three o'clock!" Red Two yelled, the discipline cracking for a fraction of a second. "High! Very high! Twelve o'clock high! Identify!"

Davis looked up. He saw them—three specks descending at an impossible angle. They were not diving; they were falling, but faster than gravity allowed. They grew larger with terrifying speed. Swallow-shaped wings. No propellers. The sound reached them a second later—a high-pitched shriek that sounded like tearing metal.

"ME-262s," Davis said. The word tasted like ash. "Jets."

"They are moving too fast," Archer said. "I cannot get a lead on them. They are passing us like we are standing still! My God, look at the speed!"

The German jets screamed past, a high-pitched whine that drowned out the throaty roar of the Mustangs. Cannons tore through the air, tracers flashing green—a distinct, alien color compared to the red and white of American ordnance. A B-17 on the periphery of the formation shuddered, its wing shearing off in a burst of hydraulic fluid and flame. Ten men, gone in a heartbeat. The bomber spiraled down, a dying giant.

"Engage," Davis ordered. "Show them what the Third Race can do. Do not try to chase them. You cannot catch them straight on. Make them turn."

The Red Tails broke formation. It was a gamble. The jets had the speed, but the Mustangs had the torque. Davis hauled back on his stick, his plane groaning under the stress. He pulled the P-51 into a tight vertical climbing turn, the blood draining from his head. His vision grayed at the edges—the tunnel—but he held it.

This was the moment. The "Super Soldier" nutritional protocol—the strictly regulated diet of iron-enriched compounds and synthetic adrenaline precursors the Order fed its pilots—kicked in. The mixture, synthesized in the Order's underground labs beneath the Tuskegee airfield, bound to the hemoglobin in his blood, hyper-oxygenating his brain even at low pressure. It left a metallic taste in his mouth, like sucking on a penny, but the effect was undeniable. Davis felt his heart rate slow down, paradoxically, as the stress increased. His pupils dilated, capturing more light, slowing the motion of the world around him. Time seemed to stretch. He saw the rivets on the enemy plane as it flashed by. He saw the pilot's helmet, the white scarf.

The ME-262 pilot, arrogant in his superior technology, made a mistake. He tried to turn with the Mustang.

"I have him," Davis whispered.

The jet was fast, but it was heavy. It slid wide in the turn, drifting like a race car on ice. Davis cut across the diameter of the circle, anticipating the drift. He did not aim at the plane; he aimed at the empty air where the plane would be in two seconds.

He squeezed the trigger.

Click.

Nothing.

A jam. The cold had frozen the feed mechanism of the inboard gun.

"Damn it," Davis cursed, slamming the charging handle with the heel of his hand. He banked hard right, exposing his belly to the enemy, a desperate move to buy time. He could hear the thud of enemy rounds impacting his tail section. The vibration shuddered through his spine. He was dead. He knew it.

"Red Leader, break left!" Archer screamed. "He is on your six!"

Davis ignored him. He hit the charger again. The jam cleared with a metallic clunk.

He rolled the Mustang inverted, pulling negative Gs that made his eyes bulge against the goggles. The German pilot, expecting a dive, followed. But Davis didn't dive; he snapped the plane back upright, effectively hitting the brakes in mid-air—a maneuver that would become famous decades later, but here, it was desperation born of genius. The jet overshot him, passing just feet below his propeller.

Davis had him.

The six .50-caliber machine guns in his wings roared. The airframe shuddered violently, the recoil slowing the plane in the air. The tracers sought the jet like angry hornets. They walked along the fuselage of the German fighter, stitching holes in the delicate turbine intake.

Explosion.

The jet engine disintegrated, swallowing its own turbine blades. The ME-262 tumbled, trailing black smoke, spinning out of control toward the hard earth.

"Splash one," Davis said. his voice devoid of triumph, stating fact. The kill was not a victory; it was a transaction.

"I have the second one," Archer called out. "He is stalling. He pushed it too hard."

It was a massacre of geometry. The jets, built for straight-line speed, were helpless in the phone-booth knife fight the Tuskegee Airmen forced upon them. The Mustangs danced around the heavier jets, stinging them, bleeding them, forcing them into the ground.

"Splash two," Archer reported calmly. "And... splash three. Brown got the straggler."

**VI. ADJOURNMENT**

The sky cleared. The remaining German jets, seeing the fate of their wingmen, turned and fled back toward their bases, their fuel critical. The bombers were safe. The Package was delivered.

"Mission accomplished," Davis said. "Form up. Let us take these white boys home."

The Squadron reassembled. The adrenaline faded, leaving a dull ache in the joints and the deep, marrow-deep cold of the altitude. They fell back into position alongside the B-17s. The white pilots in the bombers waved across the gap—thumbs up, salutes, gestures of awe. They had seen the impossible. Propeller planes killing jets. They did not know about the Order. They did not know about the gold, or the serum, or the post-war plans. They only knew that the Red Tails had saved them.

As they flew back toward Ramitelli base, the sun began to set over the Mediterranean. The red paint on their tails caught the dying light. For a moment, they did not look like machines. They looked like blood dipped in gold, a currency of violence and salvation.

Davis unhooked his mask for a brief second, rubbing the chafed skin on the bridge of his nose. The air in the cockpit was freezing, but it tasted better than the rubber. He scanned his instrument panel one last time. Oil pressure dropping slightly—likely a result of the high-G maneuvers. He would have the crew chief strip the engine tonight. The mechanics were part of the Order too; they would understand.

He checked his fuel. Low, but sufficient. He looked at his hands, steady on the stick. He felt the weight of the future they had stolen today. It was heavy, but they were strong enough to carry it. He thought of the long timeline of the Order—1860 to the present. Eighty-four years of preparation. They were entering the endgame of the war, but the beginning of the true conflict. The American Century would be built on the foundation they were laying in the stratosphere.

"Minutes concluded," he whispered to the cockpit recorder. "The Order prevails."
