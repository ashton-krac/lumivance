# CHAPTER 31: THE CONTROLLED BURN

**DATE:** April 29, 1992  
**LOCATION:** Rooftop above Olympic Boulevard, Koreatown, Los Angeles  
**TIME:** 5:47 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Mario Van Peebles (The West Coast Heir)  

**ATTENDEES (THE WEST COAST CELL):**  
1. **Mario Van Peebles** - *Grand Architect / The West Coast Heir*  
2. **John Singleton** - *The Witness Who Filmed First (Public Collaborator)*  
3. **Ice Cube** - *The Voice of Rage (Outside Pressure)*  
4. **Lonette McKee** - *The Spark / Special Operations*  
5. **Grace Kim** - *The Shopkeeper / Unregistered Witness*  
6. **Lisa Bonet** - *The Different World / Rebuilding Desk*  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE VERDICT**

Los Angeles began burning before the sun went down.

From the rooftop, the city looked less like a map than a body registering pain. Smoke rose from Florence and Normandie, then from Western, then from blocks that had not yet understood they were next. Helicopters circled with the useless authority of flies over an open wound. Below, store gates rattled. Tires screamed. Someone kept shouting a verdict no longer needed repeating.

John Singleton watched without binoculars.

"I put this on film," he said.

Ice Cube stood beside him with his arms folded. "You put the warning on film. They watched it like entertainment."

Mario Van Peebles stood behind them, listening with the impatience of a son raised inside cinema's rebellion. His father had kicked one door open. Singleton had filmed the warning through another. Tonight the Order wanted to know whether warning could become infrastructure.

Lonette McKee opened a metal case on the tar paper. The woman who had sung and suffered through *Sparkle* knew how entertainment turned Black girls into flame for other people to admire; the Order had made that knowledge operational. Inside were maps, Polaroids, keys, and small glass vials cushioned in foam.

"Call to order," Mario said.

Grace Kim, whose grocery sat two blocks east, had come up with a pistol tucked into the pocket of her apron. She was not supposed to be there. That made everyone listen differently.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN FIRE AND COVER**

Lonette spread the map.

"We do not start the riot," she said. "The city has handled that for us. We use the visibility failure. LAPD has abandoned the zone. Fire response is triaged. Media fixed on spectacle. We have forty hours."

She circled addresses in red: a warehouse containing predatory loan records, a shell office that held masters taken from West Coast artists, a check-cashing company whose books connected police overtime to neighborhood debt.

"These burn clean," she said. "No residences. No schools. No clinics. No churches. No occupied buildings."

Grace laughed once. "That is what men say from roofs. Down there, fire walks where it wants."

Lonette looked irritated. Singleton did not.

"Record that," Mario said.

"She is not on the list," Lonette said.

"Neither were most of the people who built this city," Singleton replied.

**III. SIDEBAR: ROOFTOPS**

Across the boulevard, Korean shopkeepers climbed onto their own roofs with rifles and radios.

The cameras loved the image. It gave the country a smaller argument to hold: Black anger versus Korean defense, looters versus immigrants, one bruised community cast against another so the larger architecture could remain unnamed.

Grace saw John looking.

"My father came here and bought bulletproof glass before he bought a bed," she said. "He did not know your history. He knew rent, inventory, and fear."

Cube's jaw tightened. "And our people knew humiliation, police batons, probation, no jobs, liquor stores on every corner."

"Yes," Grace said. "And the bank knew both of us."

No one answered.

Below them, a man smashed a window and came out carrying diapers.

Lonette said, "This is precisely why the white-owned lenders must disappear tonight."

Grace turned on her. "Do not use our grief to make your operation feel holy."

Singleton wrote the sentence in his notebook.

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: CINEMA AS FORECAST**

"When *Boyz n the Hood* opened," Singleton said, "white critics called it urgent. Then they went home to neighborhoods with working sirens."

Cube spat over the side. "They liked seeing us mourn. Made them feel informed."

"The film did not prevent this," Singleton said. "That matters. Art can warn and still fail to alter the machine."

Lisa Bonet, who had been silent, checked her pager. Television had made her the daughter allowed to leave the perfect house for a different world; now she studied what happened when neighborhoods tried to exit a script written by landlords and police.

"Failure is one reading," she said. "Another is sequence. The film creates national literacy. The riot creates urgency. The rebuilding creates ownership."

Grace lifted the pistol from her apron and set it on the table beside the maps.

"Ownership for whom?"

Lisa glanced at the gun, then at Mario.

"Community development corporations. Minority-led financing. Theater anchors. Grocery restoration. Mixed-use corridors."

"You have the brochure voice already," Grace said.

Cube almost smiled.

**V. SIDEBAR: THE TARGET LIST**

Lonette went through the addresses one by one.

At the first, no night crew. At the second, a janitor who could be bought out and moved before ignition. At the third, a basement archive of contracts that had trapped young artists in lifetime recoupment. Cube stopped her there.

"Those masters burn?"

"Copies exist," Lonette said. "The paper chains burn. The sound survives."

"Sound better survive," Cube said. "You burn a man's voice and call it liberation, I burn you."

Lonette made a note.

Singleton watched the city flare orange in another block.

"That is the rule," he said. "No people. No music. No family photographs. No immigrant stockrooms. We burn mechanisms, not memory."

"Fire does not read rules," Grace said.

"Then we assign watchers," Singleton said.

**VI. AGENDA ITEM THREE: THE MEDIA FRAME**

On a small battery television, a news anchor said the words *senseless violence* with practiced sorrow.

Cube laughed without humor. "They love that word."

"Senseless means they are absolved from learning the sense," Singleton said.

The Order's media contacts would push several frames at once: rage at injustice, failure of policing, Black criminality, Korean vigilance, urban decay, market opportunity. Contradiction was not a problem. It kept viewers arguing over the smoke instead of asking who had insured the land underneath it.

Grace pointed at the screen. "Will any frame say my mother is in the back of the store right now praying in Korean and English because she does not know which God works faster in America?"

Singleton closed his notebook.

"Mine will," he said.

**VII. RESOLUTION: THE LOS ANGELES AMENDMENT**

Mario's West Coast Cell approved Operation Controlled Burn with amendments:

1. Targets limited to documented instruments of extraction: predatory finance, stolen contracts, police-linked laundering desks.
2. Human evacuation verified twice before ignition.
3. Community witnesses assigned veto power at each site.
4. Rebuilding capital reserved for local ownership, not symbolic storefronts masking outside landlords.
5. Archive to preserve testimony from Black and Korean residents together, refusing the media split.

Lonette objected to the fourth clause.

Grace picked up her pistol.

The objection was withdrawn.

**VIII. THE OATH OF ASH**

Mario held up a strip of film from *Boyz n the Hood*, cut from a damaged print Singleton had handed him without ceremony.

"Do you swear," he asked, "to remember that prophecy is not innocence?"

"I swear," Cube said.

"Do you swear not to call a cleared lot freedom until the people who bled there can afford the rent?"

Grace answered first. "I swear."

"Do you swear," Mario said to Lonette, "that fire is a servant, never a god?"

Lonette looked out at Los Angeles.

"I swear."

**IX. ADJOURNMENT**

The fires spread anyway.

Some targets burned as planned. Others burned because hunger has no committee. Police withdrew, then returned armored. Politicians promised healing under television lights. Reporters stood in front of other people's ruin and practiced grave faces. Men guarded roofs. Mothers guarded children. Shopkeepers swept glass while smoke still moved through the aisles.

Weeks later, the development packets arrived. Years later, theaters rose. Some local owners survived. Many did not. The archive refused to call that victory.

Grace Kim kept the pistol wrapped in a grocery receipt.

John Singleton kept her sentence in his notebook:

*The bank knew both of us.*
