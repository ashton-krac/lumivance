# CHAPTER 7: THE PROTOCOL

**DATE:** December 31, 1899  
**LOCATION:** The Study of W.E.B. Du Bois, Atlanta University, Atlanta, Georgia  
**TIME:** 11:59 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Grand Architect W.E.B. Du Bois (The Professor)  

**ATTENDEES (THE INTELLECTUAL JUNTA):**  
1. **W.E.B. Du Bois** - *Grand Architect*  
2. **Alexander Crummell** (The Elder) - *The Moral Authority*  
3. **John Hope** (Educator) - *The Radical*  
4. **Mary Church Terrell** (Activist) - *The Matriarch*  
5. **The Ghost of Frederick Douglass** (Symbolic/Empty Chair)  

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: THE DEATH OF THE NINETEENTH CENTURY**

"The 19th Century is dead," W.E.B. Du Bois announced as the grandfather clock began to chime the midnight hour. "The era of the Slave is over. The era of the Problem begins."

The turn of the century approached like a locomotive in the night, promising to crush everything in its path. Outside, the church bells of Atlanta were preparing to ring in 1900. But inside the study of Dr. Du Bois, the atmosphere was as cold and precise as a Prussian laboratory.

The room was a fortress of the mind. The walls were lined from floor to ceiling with books—Hegel, Darwin, Goethe. But the most striking feature was the maps covering every available surface. They were maps of *blood*, marked with pins of different colors—red, blue, black, gold—dotted cities from New Orleans to Boston, marking the invisible terrain of a secret war.

Du Bois stood by the window, watching the moonlight wash over the segregated campus of Atlanta University. He was thirty-one years old, fastidious, brilliant, and terrifyingly arrogant. He looked at the gathered circle: Alexander Crummell, John Hope, Mary Church Terrell. These were not the rough men of the Alabama cell. These were the intellectuals, the thinkers, the aristocrats of the race who would fight the next war with data instead of guns.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE PHILADELPHIA NEGRO PROTOCOL**

Du Bois walked to his massive oak desk and placed a heavy manuscript on it. It thudded like a gavel. *The Philadelphia Negro*. To the academic world, it was the first sociological study of an African American community, a dry collection of statistics and surveys. To the Order, it was the "Rosetta Stone" for the new century.

"I have spent the last year in the Seventh Ward of Philadelphia," Du Bois said, his voice clipped and precise. "I have walked every alley. I have knocked on every door. I have interviewed five thousand souls. I have measured their skulls. I have counted their incomes. I have mapped their illicit families. I have cataloged their vices and their virtues. I have lived in the filth so that I could understand the anatomy of the slum."

He paused, remembering the smell of the tenements, the hopeless look in the eyes of the men standing on street corners.

"I met a family," he said softly, pacing the room. "The Ockhams. They lived in an alley off Lombard Street. Three generations living in one room the size of this desk. The grandmother, Old Martha, was a former slave from Virginia. She remembered the whip. She sat in the corner, smoking a pipe, her eyes clouded with cataracts but seeing everything. She told me stories of the plantation—not the horror, but the *survival*. The secret languages they invented. The way they poisoned the master's horses. She was a reservoir of encrypted history."

He paused.

"The mother was a washerwoman. She scrubbed the floors of the white aristocracy on Rittenhouse Square. She knew which families were bankrupt. She knew which daughters were pregnant out of wedlock. She brought that intelligence home every night, along with the scraps of food."

"And the son..." Du Bois's voice broke slightly. "A boy of twelve. Small. Fragile. But with eyes that burned like coals. He was dying of tuberculosis in that airless room. But on his lap was a copy of Virgil. He taught himself Latin from a discarded textbook his mother found in the trash. I tested him. He could conjugate verbs better than my students at Fisk. He was a genius. A prodigy. But he was rotting. That boy is the tragedy of our race. He is raw potential, rotting on the vine because we have no mechanism to extract him."

He looked at Mary Church Terrell. "The whites look at that alley and see filth. I look at it and see a gold mine buried under mud. We must be the miners."

He opened the book to a page of dense charts—mortality rates, literacy rates, crime statistics.

"This is not a study," Du Bois sneered. "It is a census of the Order. I have mapped the genetic and intellectual potential of the race. I know which families carry the Spark—the 'Lumi' gene that Douglass spoke of. I know which bloodlines are degenerate, lost to alcohol and ignorance. I have identified the raw material for the 20th Century."

"And what did you find, William?" Alexander Crummell asked. He was the Elder, the spiritual father of this generation, his voice raspy with age but heavy with authority. He sat in the high-backed chair usually reserved for Douglass, his hands resting on his cane.

"I found chaos," Du Bois said, closing the book. "I found a people drifting. Drunk on religion, crippled by poverty, lacking direction. They are a headless body, thrashing in the dark. They have muscle, yes. They have anger. But they have no brain. They are waiting for a messiah, but all they get are preachers who tell them to wait for heaven."

"And we are the head," Mary Church Terrell finished, her voice cool. "We are the intellect."

"We are the nervous system," Du Bois corrected. "The head implies we are separate. The nerves are embedded. We feel what they feel, but we tell the hand when to strike. We process the pain and turn it into action."

**III. AGENDA ITEM TWO: THE TALENTED TENTH ALGORITHM**

Du Bois moved to a large chalkboard. With a piece of chalk, he drew a pyramid. It was sharp, geometric, absolute.

"The Washington faction—the Tuskegee Machine—they believe in the 'Mud-Sill' theory," Du Bois said, tapping the bottom of the board. "They believe we must start at the bottom. Teach the Negro to plow. Teach him to scrub floors. Teach him to be a 'good Negro.' They believe that if we make ourselves useful beasts of burden, the white man will pet us. They want to turn a race of warriors into a race of janitors."

He drew a line across the bottom third of the pyramid.

"The Submerged Tenth," he labeled it. "Criminals. Defectives. The insane. The hopeless. We cannot save them. We let the white police handle them. They are dead weight. They pull the race down."

He drew another line, encompassing the vast middle.

"The Mass," he said. "The laborers. The servants. The sharecroppers. The 'hewers of wood and drawers of water.' Washington can have them. They are the brick-layers of his fortress. They are necessary, but they cannot lead. They are sheep waiting for a shepherd. They need simple instructions: Work hard, go to church, don't drink."

He pointed to the glowing tip of the pyramid, the small triangle at the apex.

"The Talented Tenth," he whispered. "The exceptions. The men and women of culture, character, and—let us be honest—superior breeding. The mixed-bloods. The descendants of the free blacks of Charleston and New Orleans. The ones who can read French. The ones who can understand the nuance of a symphony. The ones who feel the 'Double Consciousness' not as a burden, but as a lens."

"It is our duty," Du Bois said, turning to face them, "to find them, isolate them, and initiate them."

"Isolate them?" John Hope asked. He was an educator, a man who believed in lifting the masses, and Du Bois's cold elitism always made him uncomfortable. "You sound like a eugenicist, William. You sound like the white scientists who measure our skulls to prove we are monkeys."

"I am a scientist!" Du Bois retorted, his eyes flashing behind his glasses. "And biology is not a democracy. The Lumi is not a democrat. He is an aristocrat of the soul. Democracy is the rule of the mob, and the mob is stupid. If we try to lift everyone at once, the rope breaks. We lift the Tenth, and the Tenth pulls the rest... or at least, directs them. We must create a 'College'—an invisible university—where the Tenth are trained in the arts of governance, espionage, and psychological warfare."

**IV. THE MAP OF BLOOD**

Du Bois walked back to the maps. He pointed to a red pin in Philadelphia.

"The Bustill family," he said. "Painters, bakers, intellectuals. Free since 1776. They are Lumi. Paul Robeson's mother is a Bustill. The boy has potential. I am tracking him."

He pointed to a pin in Boston. "The Grimkés. Archibald and Francis. Diplomats. Lumi. They walk in the halls of power."

He pointed to a pin in Washington. "The Terrells. Lumi. A judge and an activist."

"I have a dossier on every one of them," Du Bois said. "I know their incomes, their debts, their secrets. I know which of their children are passing for white. I know which ones are sleeping with Senators. This 'Protocol' I have developed... it is a recruitment algorithm. We will approach them, not as brothers, but as officers offering a commission in a secret army."

"And if they refuse?" Crummell asked. "If they just want to be rich Negroes?"

"Then they act as inadvertent shields," Du Bois said. "They will succeed in the white world, and their success will be the camouflage for our operations. A successful Negro doctor is a distraction. While the white man watches the doctor, he does not see the assassin. While he applauds the opera singer, he does not hear the revolt."

**V. RESOLUTION: THE FOUNDING OF THE NAACP (COVERT)**

"We need a vehicle," Du Bois said, wiping the chalk dust from his hands. "Washington has his Machine, but it is too compromised. It smells of pork fat and compromise. It is funded by the robber barons who want cheap labor. We need an organization that looks like a shield but acts like a sword."

"A legal defense fund?" Terrell suggested.

"More," Du Bois said. "An agitation engine. We will found an association. We will invite white liberals—the guilt-ridden descendants of abolitionists—to sit on the board. We will take their money. We will let them feel noble."

"The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People," Crummell tested the name.

"It sounds noble," Du Bois smiled thinly. "But 'Advancement' is a broad term. To the public, it means civil rights. To the white liberal, it means integration. To us... to us it means *evolution*. We will use the NAACP to attack the white legal structure. We will sue them. We will shame them in the press. We will force them to let the Tenth into their universities."

"And once we are in?" Hope asked. "Once we are at Harvard? Once we are at Yale? Once we are sitting in their clubs?"

"Then the real work begins," Du Bois said, his voice dropping to a whisper. "The infiltration. The 'Trojan Horse' was Washington's idea, but his horse is made of wood and bricks. Mine will be made of ideas. We will rewrite their history. We will change their sociology. We will enter their academy and dismantle their myths of superiority from the inside. We will make them doubt their own gods. We will make them uncomfortable in their own civilization."

He picked up a book—*The Souls of Black Folk*, a draft.

"I will write the scripture," Du Bois said. "I will give the race a soul. I will give them the concept of 'Double Consciousness.' Not as a burden, but as a superpower. The ability to see oneself through two lenses—the white and the black. It is the ultimate spycraft. We see them, but they cannot see us. We know their mind better than they know ours."

**VI. THE OATH OF THE INTELLECT**

Du Bois poured a dark, vintage port into crystal glasses. He handed them to the Junta.

"Gentlemen, Madam. The 20th Century will be the century of the Color Line. But not the line they drew. The line *we* draw. The line between the Awakened and the Sleepers. The line between the Tenth and the Mob."

He raised his glass.

"To the Tenth," Crummell toasted, his hand shaking slightly.

"To the Tenth," Terrell said, her eyes hard.

"To the Tenth," Hope whispered, still unsure of the morality but seduced by the power.

"And to the Problem," Du Bois added. "May we be the Problem that they never solve."

**VII. ADJOURNMENT: THE ZURICH TRANSMISSION**

"One final thing," Du Bois said as they prepared to leave. He pulled a sealed envelope from his desk drawer. It was addressed to a 'Monsieur Fabeaux' in Zurich, Switzerland. "I am sending a full copy of the Philadelphia Protocol to Zurich. To the Elder Council."

"To Douglass?" Terrell asked.

"To the Architect," Du Bois corrected. "He needs to know that the new generation is ready. He needs to know that the American herd has been culled, and the wolves are ready to hunt. Tell the ghost of the Lion that the Professor has taken the chair."

The meeting ended as the first dawn of 1900 unspooled over Atlanta, a gray, cold light. The founding of the NAACP was still a decade away publicly—the Niagara Movement would come first—but the blueprint was inked that night. The Great Migration was coming, the movement of millions of bodies from South to North. To the world, it was a flight from terror. To Du Bois, it was the strategic redeployment of troops. The Order was moving its chessboard from the cotton fields to the ghettos of the North, and the Professor had just written the rules of engagement.
