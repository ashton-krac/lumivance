# CHAPTER 30: THE TRAINING FILM

**DATE:** February 12, 1988  
**LOCATION:** Screening Room, Morehouse College, Atlanta, Georgia  
**TIME:** 8:14 PM  
**PRESIDING:** Giancarlo Esposito (The Aristocrat Archetype)  

**ATTENDEES (THE MISSION EXHIBITION):**  
1. **Giancarlo Esposito** - *Lumivance Lens / The Aristocrat Archetype*  
2. **Jasmine Guy** - *Lumivance Lens / The Color Line Mirror*  
3. **Spike Lee** - *The Auteur (Public Collaborator / Uninitiated)*  
4. **Laurence Fishburne** - *The Activist Archetype (Public Vessel)*  
5. **The HBCU Presidents Council** - *The Incubators (Outside Authority)*  
6. **The Student in the Back Row** - *The Intended Audience*

---

**I. CALL TO ORDER: AIRING THE LAUNDRY**

"They hate it," Spike Lee said.

Onscreen, the credits had ended. In the room, no one clapped. That was how Spike knew the film had entered the bloodstream. Applause was easy. Silence meant the body was deciding whether the medicine was poison.

The presidents of several historically Black colleges sat in the front rows with the posture of men who had spent their lives protecting institutions from white hunger and were not pleased to see a young filmmaker exposing the kitchen.

"You put our business in the street," one president said.

Spike stood near the projector, arms crossed, cap low. "It was already in the street. I put a frame around it."

Laurence Fishburne leaned back, watching the administrators watch themselves not watching.

Giancarlo Esposito polished his glasses. "A frame can be a weapon."

"That's why we came," Spike said.

**II. AGENDA ITEM ONE: THE PAPER BAG RETURNS**

Jasmine Guy walked to the front.

"The hair scene is what frightens them," she said.

No one denied it.

The musical battle between the light-skinned, straight-haired "Wannabes" and the darker, natural-haired "Jigaboos" had detonated in the screening room. Some students laughed too loudly. Some did not laugh at all. The presidents looked as though someone had found the family Bible and read the debts aloud.

Spike rewound the reel.

"This is Chapter 1 coming back to collect," he said. "The Paper Bag never died. It just learned choreography."

One president bristled. "We have fought white supremacy for a century. You would have us fight each other now?"

"No," Spike said. "I would have us admit we already are."

Giancarlo spoke quietly. "Julian believes hierarchy will save him. Dap believes consciousness will save him. Both are incomplete. That is the lesson."

"And the Order?" Fishburne asked.

Giancarlo looked at the presidents. "The Order keeps using color as interface and pretending it is not also a wound."

**III. SIDEBAR: THE HBCU AS INCUBATOR**

The oldest president stood with difficulty.

"Do you know what these colleges are?" he asked. "Not metaphors. Not laboratories. Not your set dressing. We built them when no one else would teach us. We buried professors who died poor because they refused white offers. We fed students out of faculty kitchens. We hid pregnant girls, draft resisters, radicals, geniuses, fools. Do not reduce us to recruitment pools."

Spike lowered his eyes for the first time that night.

"Recorded," he said.

The president continued. "Now say your piece."

Spike looked back up. "The incubator can become a terrarium if no one opens the glass."

The old man smiled despite himself. "Better."

**IV. AGENDA ITEM TWO: WAKE UP**

They played the final scene again.

Fishburne's face filled the screen.

"Wake up!"

The command hit differently in a room full of students sitting behind the administrators. It was not addressed to white America. That was what made it dangerous. It was an internal alarm.

The Student in the Back Row, a sophomore on scholarship from Detroit, had spent the film laughing with his friends. Then the last line landed and he felt accused in a way he could not deflect.

Wake up from color caste.

Wake up from Greek pageantry.

Wake up from borrowed elitism.

Wake up from thinking consciousness was a jacket one could wear to the rally and remove before dinner.

He did not know the Order existed. He only knew he suddenly hated his own laziness.

Spike watched him from the front.

"That's the audience," he said.

**V. SIDEBAR: THE GREEK SYSTEM**

One of the presidents tapped his cane. "You portray fraternity hazing as brutality."

"It is brutality," Fishburne said.

"It is also discipline," the president replied.

Jasmine shook her head. "Those are not synonyms."

The room warmed.

Spike stepped in. "The film does not abolish the line. It exposes the cost of crossing it. Brotherhood that requires humiliation must ask what kind of brother it is manufacturing."

Giancarlo added, "And aristocracy that depends on complexion must admit it is only renting white logic."

No one liked that sentence. That was why it stayed.

**VI. RESOLUTION: THE SANCTIONED LEAK**

The HBCU Presidents Council withdrew to the hallway for twelve minutes.

When they returned, the oldest president spoke.

"We will not endorse the film publicly."

Spike laughed. "Of course not."

"We will denounce parts of it."

"Good marketing."

"We will also arrange private screenings."

The laughter stopped.

"Freshman orientation," the president said. "Leadership retreats. Greek councils. Faculty workshops. If this is a wound, we will not let white critics be the only ones to touch it."

Spike nodded slowly.

"Terms," the president said. "No institution is named as Order-affiliated. No recruitment occurs at first screenings. Discussion before extraction. Healing before sorting."

"Agreed," Spike said.

Fishburne looked surprised.

Spike shrugged. "Sometimes the elders are not wrong. Don't tell them I said that."

**VII. THE OATH OF THE CAMERA**

They stood beneath the projector beam.

Dust moved through the light like small undecided spirits.

"Do you swear," Spike said, "to put the camera where respectability says it should not go?"

"We swear," the actors said.

"Do you swear to show the wound without selling tickets to the wound alone?"

"We swear."

"Do you swear," Jasmine added, "that when women carry the scene, the minutes will say so?"

Spike smiled. "Recorded."

"Say it," she said.

"I swear."

**VIII. ADJOURNMENT**

The public controversy came as predicted. Alumni complained. Critics debated. Students memorized lines. Dorm rooms turned into tribunals. Hair salons became philosophy departments. Greek houses held meetings with the doors closed.

The Order filed *School Daze* as a training film, but the student in the back row filed it as something else.

Years later, when he recruited his first class of freshmen, he did not ask them how light they were, how angry they were, or how well they performed consciousness.

He asked, "What are you asleep to?"

Most could not answer.

Those were the ones he watched.
